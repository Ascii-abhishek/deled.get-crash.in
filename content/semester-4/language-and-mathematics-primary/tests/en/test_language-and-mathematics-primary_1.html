Section 2: Short Answer Questions (10 Questions)

1. Write the short meaning of Language Skills.

Language learning develops listening, speaking, reading, and writing through meaningful use of sounds, words, sentences, and texts. It can be explained through definition, purpose, and classroom use.

2. Write the short meaning of Study of Sound.

Sound study builds listening, phonemic awareness, pronunciation, rhythm, and expressive oral language. It can be explained through definition, purpose, and classroom use.

3. Write the short meaning of Reading and Writing in the Pure Form of Script.

Script learning includes letter recognition, matras, punctuation, spacing, directionality, nasal sounds, and meaningful writing practice. It can be explained through definition, purpose, and classroom use.

4. Write the short meaning of Early Numeracy.

Early numeracy builds number sense through counting, one-to-one correspondence, comparison, ordering, grouping, and symbols. It can be explained through definition, purpose, and classroom use.

5. Write the short meaning of Mathematical Operations.

Operations grow from concrete actions: joining, separating, repeated addition, sharing, grouping, zero, and place value. It can be explained through definition, purpose, and classroom use.

6. Write one difference related to Listening and Speaking.

Decoding means reading symbols aloud; comprehension means understanding the message.

7. Write one difference related to Sound and Pronunciation.

Hearing is physical reception of sound; listening is attentive understanding.

8. Write one difference related to Script and Punctuation.

Copying trains form; independent writing trains thought and expression.

9. Write one difference related to Number sense and One-to-one correspondence.

Counting is saying number names; number sense is understanding quantity and relation.

10. Write one difference related to Addition and Subtraction.

Repeated addition leads to multiplication; repeated subtraction can lead to division.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Explain the main concepts of Language Skills with examples.
  • Language learning develops listening, speaking, reading, and writing through meaningful use of sounds, words, sentences, and texts.
  • The four skills develop together; oral language supports reading and writing.
  • Children learn language best through stories, songs, conversation, play, and purposeful print.
  • Letters should be linked with sounds and meaningful words, not taught as isolated marks only.
  • Classroom example: Use a picture of a market: children listen to a story, name objects, read labels, and write two sentences.
2. Explain the main concepts of Study of Sound with examples.
  • Sound study builds listening, phonemic awareness, pronunciation, rhythm, and expressive oral language.
  • Listening comes before formal reading and supports correct pronunciation.
  • Poems, rhymes, folk songs, and stories develop sound patterns naturally.
  • Children need repeated but joyful practice for difficult sounds and blends.
  • Classroom example: Play a sound hunt: children find classroom words beginning with क, clap syllables, and recite a short rhyme.
3. Explain the main concepts of Reading and Writing in the Pure Form of Script with examples.
  • Script learning includes letter recognition, matras, punctuation, spacing, directionality, nasal sounds, and meaningful writing practice.
  • Children need large-to-small movement practice before neat notebook writing.
  • Letter shape, direction, matra placement, spacing, and punctuation should be taught together with meaning.
  • Reading and writing should use familiar words, labels, names, and short messages.
  • Classroom example: Create a class word wall from children's names, local objects, and story words; use it for reading and sentence writing.
4. Explain the main concepts of Early Numeracy with examples.
  • Early numeracy builds number sense through counting, one-to-one correspondence, comparison, ordering, grouping, and symbols.
  • Children first build quantity ideas through objects, movement, stories, and games.
  • Rote counting is not enough; learners must connect number words, objects, and symbols.
  • Numbers 1 to 9 should be taught through grouping, comparison, and local examples.
  • Classroom example: Use seeds, bottle caps, and attendance sticks for counting, matching, making groups, and comparing numbers.
5. Explain the main concepts of Mathematical Operations with examples.
  • Operations grow from concrete actions: joining, separating, repeated addition, sharing, grouping, zero, and place value.
  • Begin with concrete material, then pictures, then symbols.
  • Addition and subtraction should include joining, taking away, comparison, and missing-number situations.
  • Multiplication should begin with equal groups, arrays, skip counting, and repeated addition.
  • Classroom example: Use bundles of ten sticks and loose sticks to show 34 + 18, then connect the action to written addition.
