Presentation of Data and Measures of Central Tendency

This crash-course chapter brings together the core definitions, comparisons, classroom uses, and likely exam lines for D.El.Ed revision.

Exam Snapshot

TermExam meaningExample
DataFacts or numbers collected for studyMarks, height, rainfall
TabulationArranging data in rows and columnsFrequency table
Bar graphGraph using equal-width barsClass-wise attendance
MeanSum of values divided by number of valuesAverage marks
MedianMiddle value after arranging dataCentral score

Key Points

  • Statistics helps collect, organise, present, analyse and interpret data.
  • Raw data should be arranged before drawing conclusions.
  • Tables make comparison quick; graphs make patterns visible.
  • Mean is affected by extreme values; median is useful for ordered data with extremes.
  • Mode is the most frequent value and helps identify common preference.
  • Choice of graph depends on data type: bar graph for categories, line graph for change over time, pie chart for parts of whole.

Classroom Connection

  • Collect class attendance for a week and draw a bar graph.
  • Use marks of five students to calculate mean, median and mode.
  • Discuss how misleading graphs can create wrong impressions.

One-minute Revision

  • Data must be organised before interpretation.
  • Tables and graphs communicate data clearly.
  • Mean = total / number of observations.
  • Median is the middle value after ordering.
  • Mode is the value with highest frequency.

Common Exam Lines

  • Explain tabulation.
