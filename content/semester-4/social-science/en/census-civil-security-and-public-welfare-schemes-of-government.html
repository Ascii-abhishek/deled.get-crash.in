Census, Civil Security and Public Welfare Schemes of Government

This crash-course chapter brings together the core definitions, comparisons, classroom uses, and likely exam lines for D.El.Ed revision.

Key Points

  • Census data helps planning for schools, roads, hospitals, ration shops and employment programmes.
  • Civil security includes warning, rescue, first aid, evacuation and rehabilitation.
  • Welfare schemes try to reduce poverty, hunger, unemployment and social exclusion.
  • Public distribution, mid-day meal, ICDS, pensions and scholarships are common welfare examples.
  • NGOs support government work through awareness, training, surveys and community mobilisation.
  • Transparency needs records, beneficiary lists, grievance redressal and social audit.

Classroom Connection

  • Use a mock village census to teach data collection and privacy.
  • Prepare a classroom disaster map with exits, safe corners and first-aid roles.
  • Discuss how a school meal or scholarship reaches the child and where records matter.

One-minute Revision

  • Census is the base of evidence-based planning.
  • Civil security means preparedness before emergency, action during emergency and rehabilitation after it.
  • Welfare schemes should reach the right beneficiary at the right time.
  • NGOs act as a bridge between state and community.
  • Social audit strengthens accountability.

Common Exam Lines

  • Define census and write its uses.
  • Explain civil security with examples.
  • Write notes on major welfare schemes.
  • What is the role of NGOs in public welfare?
  • Why is social audit important?
