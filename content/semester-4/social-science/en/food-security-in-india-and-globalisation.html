Food Security in India and Globalisation

This crash-course chapter brings together the core definitions, comparisons, classroom uses, and likely exam lines for D.El.Ed revision.

Key Points

  • Food security needs food production, purchasing power, fair distribution and nutrition awareness.
  • PDS provides grains to eligible households through ration shops.
  • FCI procures grains at support prices, stores buffer stock and supplies PDS.
  • ICDS and mid-day meal support child nutrition and school participation.
  • The food security law gives legal entitlement to subsidised grains for eligible people.
  • Globalisation expands markets and technology but may increase competition and inequality.

Classroom Connection

  • Use a food plate activity to discuss cereals, pulses, milk, fruits and vegetables.
  • Link school mid-day meal with attendance, nutrition and equality.
  • Compare local market food with packaged global products to discuss globalisation.

One-minute Revision

  • Food security has three pillars: availability, access and absorption.
  • FCI maintains buffer stock and supports PDS.
  • ICDS and mid-day meal target child nutrition.
  • Globalisation has both opportunities and risks.
  • Nutrition is as important as grain quantity.

Common Exam Lines

  • Define food security and its pillars.
  • Explain the role of FCI.
  • Write a note on PDS.
  • How do ICDS and mid-day meal help children?
  • Discuss globalisation and Indian food security.
