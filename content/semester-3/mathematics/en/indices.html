Indices

This chapter explains index notation, laws of indices, zero and negative powers, and fast simplification of repeated multiplication.

Core Points

  • Indices shorten repeated multiplication and make calculation compact.
  • Apply addition of powers only when bases are the same and terms are multiplied.
  • Apply subtraction of powers only when same bases are divided.
  • Remember that zero power gives 1, not 0, for every non-zero base.
  • Negative index means reciprocal; it does not mean negative value.
  • Use powers of 10 for place-value, measurement, and scientific notation activities.

Classroom Connection

Use folded paper or doubling charts to show powers of 2, then move to symbolic rules and error spotting.

One-minute Revision

  • Base: The number or expression repeatedly multiplied.
  • Index: The power showing how many times the base is used.
  • Exponential form: Writing repeated multiplication as a power, e.g., 2^5.
  • Product law: a^m x a^n = a^(m+n).
  • Quotient law: a^m / a^n = a^(m-n), where a is not zero.
  • Power law: (a^m)^n = a^(mn).
