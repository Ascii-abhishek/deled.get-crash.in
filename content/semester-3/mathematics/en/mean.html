Mean

This chapter explains arithmetic mean as a measure of central tendency, its calculation, interpretation, merits, limits, and classroom data activities.

Core Points

  • Mean = total of all observations / number of observations.
  • For frequency data, multiply each value by its frequency, add, then divide by total frequency.
  • Mean uses every observation, so it is stable for balanced data.
  • Mean is affected by outliers; do not interpret it blindly.
  • Mean is useful for average marks, rainfall, attendance, and daily sales.
  • Let children collect class heights, number of siblings, or reading minutes and compute mean.

Quick Memory Hook

Mean balances the total equally among all observations.

Classroom Connection

Ask groups to record how many pages they read in ten minutes. They compute group mean and discuss whether one very high value changes the result.

One-minute Revision

  • Data: Collected facts or numerical information.
  • Observation: Each value in a data set.
  • Central tendency: Single value representing a data set.
  • Mean: Sum of observations divided by number of observations.
  • Ungrouped data: Raw data values listed separately.
  • Grouped data: Data arranged in classes or frequency table.
