Carbon and Their Compound

Carbon is a special non-metal because it forms millions of compounds through tetravalency and catenation. This chapter covers carbon forms, hydrocarbons, functional groups, fuels, and everyday carbon compounds.

Core Points

  • Carbon forms covalent bonds by sharing electrons.
  • Diamond is hard and non-conducting; graphite is soft and conducts electricity.
  • Organic compounds include fuels, food, plastics, medicines, soaps, oils, and fibres.
  • Incomplete combustion produces carbon monoxide, a poisonous gas.
  • Soaps and detergents are carbon compounds used for cleaning, but excessive detergent harms water bodies.
  • Teach carbon through daily examples: LPG, candle, pencil graphite, sugar, plastic, cotton, medicines.

Quick Memory Hook

Carbon is small in atom, huge in compounds.

Classroom Connection

Use pencil lead, candle, sugar, plastic spoon, cotton thread, and LPG picture cards. Children classify carbon forms, fuels, natural materials, and synthetic materials, then discuss safe fuel use.

One-minute Revision

  • Carbon: Non-metal element with atomic number 6.
  • Tetravalency: Carbon forms four covalent bonds.
  • Catenation: Ability of carbon to bond with itself in chains/rings.
  • Allotrope: Different forms of same element with different structures.
  • Hydrocarbon: Compound containing only carbon and hydrogen.
  • Saturated compound: Carbon compound with single bonds only.
