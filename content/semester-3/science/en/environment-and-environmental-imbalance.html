Environment and Environmental Imbalance

Environment includes living and non-living surroundings. Environmental imbalance occurs when natural relationships are disturbed by pollution, deforestation, overuse, population pressure, or climate change.

Core Points

  • Human activities can disturb air, water, soil, forests, wildlife, and climate.
  • Imbalance results in floods, droughts, heat stress, disease spread, crop loss, and species decline.
  • Pollution control needs individual habits and community rules.
  • Reduce single-use plastic, segregate waste, save water, plant local trees, and protect common spaces.
  • Environmental education should be activity-based: audit, survey, plantation, compost pit, water testing.
  • Children should learn local environmental problems before global terms.

Quick Memory Hook

Environmental balance breaks slowly, but its effects arrive suddenly.

Classroom Connection

Conduct a school waste audit for two days. Sort wet waste, dry waste, paper, plastic, and hazardous items. Make a class plan for composting leaves and reducing plastic wrappers.

One-minute Revision

  • Environment: All living and non-living surroundings affecting life.
  • Ecosystem: Interaction of organisms with physical environment.
  • Environmental balance: Stable relationship among organisms and resources.
  • Environmental imbalance: Disturbance in natural balance.
  • Pollution: Addition of harmful substances to environment.
  • Deforestation: Large-scale removal of trees.
