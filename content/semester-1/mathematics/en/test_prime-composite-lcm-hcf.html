Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define prime number.

A prime number is a number greater than 1 with exactly two factors: 1 and itself.

2. Define composite number.

A composite number is a number greater than 1 with more than two factors.

3. Why is 1 neither prime nor composite?

1 has only one factor, so it does not fit the definition of prime or composite numbers.

4. What is prime factorisation?

Prime factorisation means writing a number as a product of prime factors.

5. Give one use of LCM.

LCM is used in repeated-event problems, such as finding when two bells ring together.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain prime and composite numbers.
  • Prime numbers are greater than 1 and have exactly two factors.
  • Examples are 2, 3, 5 and 7.
  • Composite numbers have more than two factors.
  • Examples are 4, 6, 8 and 9.
  • 1 is neither prime nor composite.
2. Describe methods of finding LCM and HCF.
  • Listing method uses common multiples for LCM and common factors for HCF.
  • Prime factorisation uses prime factors and their powers.
  • Division method can also be used.
  • LCM takes required highest powers of prime factors.
  • HCF takes common prime factors with lowest powers.
3. Differentiate LCM and HCF.
  • LCM is the least common multiple.
  • HCF is the highest common factor.
  • LCM is generally greater than or equal to given numbers.
  • HCF is less than or equal to given numbers.
  • LCM is used for common repetition; HCF for maximum equal grouping.
4. How should this topic be taught in primary classes?
  • Use factor discovery through arrays and division.
  • Use number charts and the sieve idea to identify primes.
  • Use factor trees for prime factorisation.
  • Give word problems for LCM and HCF separately.
  • Ask learners to explain whether a problem needs common repetition or equal grouping.
