Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define angle.

An angle is a figure formed by two rays having a common endpoint called the vertex.

2. What is an acute angle?

An acute angle is greater than 0 degrees and less than 90 degrees.

3. Give two examples of right angles in the classroom.

The corner of a notebook and the corner of a blackboard are examples of right angles.

4. What are supplementary angles?

Two angles whose measures add to 180 degrees are supplementary angles.

5. Why does arm length not change angle size?

Angle size depends on the opening between the two arms, not on how long the arms are drawn.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Classify angles according to their measurement.
  • Zero angle: 0 degrees.
  • Acute angle: more than 0 and less than 90 degrees.
  • Right angle: exactly 90 degrees.
  • Obtuse angle: more than 90 and less than 180 degrees.
  • Straight angle: exactly 180 degrees.
  • Reflex angle: more than 180 and less than 360 degrees.
  • Complete angle: exactly 360 degrees.
2. Explain complementary and supplementary angles with examples.

Complementary angles have a sum of 90 degrees, such as 30 degrees and 60 degrees. Supplementary angles have a sum of 180 degrees, such as 110 degrees and 70 degrees. These relationships help in solving unknown angle questions.

3. Write common mistakes children make while identifying angles.
  • They may think longer arms mean a bigger angle.
  • They may confuse obtuse and reflex angles.
  • They may read the wrong scale on a protractor.
  • They may forget to place the vertex at the centre of the protractor.
4. How will you teach types of angles in primary classes?
  • Begin with clock hands, doors, scissors, and book corners.
  • Use a paper right-angle checker for comparison.
  • Let learners draw and sort acute, right, and obtuse angles.
  • Use protractors only after visual understanding is strong.
  • Connect angle pairs with paper folding and straight lines.
