Basic Geometry

This chapter revises the language of geometry: point, line, line segment, ray, curve, plane, plane region and angle.

Core Concepts

  • A point is shown by a small dot and named with a capital letter.
  • A line has no fixed length; only a part can be drawn on paper.
  • A line segment can be measured because it has two end points.
  • A ray starts from one point and continues in one direction.
  • A curve may be open or closed; a simple closed curve divides the plane into interior, boundary and exterior.

Differences

Concept AConcept BMain difference
LineLine segmentLine is endless; segment has two end points.
Line segmentRaySegment has two end points; ray has one.
Open curveClosed curveOpen curve does not enclose a region; closed curve does.
PlanePlane regionPlane is endless flat surface; plane region is a bounded part.

Angles

  • An angle has two arms and one vertex.
  • The common end point of the rays is called the vertex.
  • Angles are named with three letters; the vertex letter is written in the middle.
  • Examples in the classroom include book corners, door opening and clock hands.

Classroom Connection

Ask children to find points, lines, segments, rays and angles in the classroom: corners of a notebook, edges of the blackboard, sunlight ray drawings and hands of a clock.

One-minute Revision

  • Point shows position.
  • Line is endless in two directions.
  • Line segment has two end points and measurable length.
  • Ray has one end point.
  • Angle is formed by two rays meeting at a vertex.

Common Exam Lines

  • Geometry begins with undefined ideas such as point and line, understood through models.
  • Concrete objects help children form mental images of abstract geometrical ideas.
  • Correct naming and drawing are important in elementary geometry teaching.
