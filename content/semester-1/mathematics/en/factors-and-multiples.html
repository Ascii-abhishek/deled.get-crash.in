Factors and Multiples

This chapter revises factors, divisors, multiples, common factors and common multiples as foundations for divisibility, fractions, LCM and HCF.

Factors

  • A factor divides a number without leaving a remainder.
  • Every number has 1 and itself as factors.
  • Factors of a number are finite. For example, factors of 18 are 1, 2, 3, 6, 9 and 18.
  • Factor pairs help list factors systematically: 18 = 1 x 18 = 2 x 9 = 3 x 6.

Multiples

  • Multiples are obtained by skip counting or multiplication.
  • Multiples of a number are infinite: 7, 14, 21, 28 and so on.
  • Every number is a multiple of itself.
  • Zero is a multiple of every whole number because n x 0 = 0.

Common Factors And Common Multiples

  • Common factors of 12 and 18 are 1, 2, 3 and 6.
  • Common multiples of 4 and 6 are 12, 24, 36 and so on.
  • The greatest common factor leads to HCF.
  • The least common multiple leads to LCM.

Classroom Connection

Give children counters and ask them to arrange 24 counters into equal rows. Every possible row size that leaves no counter becomes a factor of 24. Then use skip-counting charts for multiples.

One-minute Revision

  • Factors divide exactly; multiples are products.
  • Factors of a number are finite, multiples are infinite.
  • Divisibility rules save time in factor finding.
  • Common factors and multiples prepare learners for HCF and LCM.
  • Arrays and skip counting make the concepts visible.

Common Exam Lines

  • A factor of a number divides it completely without remainder.
  • A multiple of a number is obtained by multiplying it by whole numbers.
  • Primary students should discover factors through equal grouping and arrays.
