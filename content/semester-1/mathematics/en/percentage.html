Percentage

This chapter revises percentage as a way of comparing quantities out of 100, with classroom links to marks, discounts, attendance and simple data.

Core Ideas

  • Percentage gives a common base of 100, so different quantities become easy to compare.
  • 100% means the whole quantity, 50% means half, 25% means one-fourth and 10% means one-tenth.
  • To find p% of a number, use p/100 x number.
  • To find what percent one quantity is of another, use part/whole x 100.
  • Percentage is closely connected with fractions, decimals, ratio and proportion.

Methods Of Finding Percentage

  • Unitary method: If 1% of 200 is 2, then 15% of 200 is 30.
  • Fraction method: 25% of 80 = 25/100 x 80 = 20.
  • Decimal method: 12% of 150 = 0.12 x 150 = 18.
  • Comparison method: If a child scores 36 out of 40, percentage = 36/40 x 100 = 90%.

Common Errors

  • Students may treat 20% as 20 instead of 20/100. Use a hundred-square grid.
  • They may confuse "percent of" with "percent increase". Ask them to identify whole and part first.
  • They may compare raw marks without checking total marks. Convert both to percentages before comparing.

Classroom Connection

Use a 10 x 10 grid with 100 squares. Shade 25 squares for 25%, then connect it to 1/4, 0.25, attendance, marks and small shop discounts.

One-minute Revision

  • Percent means per hundred.
  • p% = p/100.
  • Part = percent/100 x whole.
  • Percentage helps compare quantities with different totals.
  • Marks, attendance, discount and data are strong teaching contexts.

Common Exam Lines

  • Percentage is a special fraction whose denominator is 100.
  • The concept of percentage should be taught through grids, money, marks and daily-life situations.
  • Before comparing two results, convert them to the same base of 100.
