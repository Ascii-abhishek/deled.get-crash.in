Plane Figures

Plane figures are flat closed shapes drawn on a plane surface. This chapter revises their parts, properties, and classroom teaching value.

Basic Terms

  • Plane figure: a two-dimensional shape with length and breadth but no thickness.
  • Closed figure: a figure whose boundary has no gap.
  • Side: a line segment forming the boundary of a polygon.
  • Vertex: point where two sides meet.
  • Diagonal: a line segment joining two non-adjacent vertices.
  • Perimeter: total boundary length of a closed figure.
  • Area: measure of surface enclosed by a closed figure.

Circle Terms

  • Centre: fixed point inside a circle.
  • Radius: distance from centre to any point on the circle.
  • Diameter: line segment passing through centre and joining two points on the circle; diameter = 2 x radius.
  • Circumference: boundary length of a circle.
  • Chord: line segment joining any two points on the circle.
  • Arc: a part of the circle boundary.

Common Difference Table

PairDifference
Square and rectangleBoth have four right angles; square has all sides equal, rectangle has opposite sides equal.
Circle and polygonCircle has curved boundary; polygon has straight line segments.
Perimeter and areaPerimeter measures boundary length; area measures enclosed surface.
Radius and diameterRadius is half the diameter; diameter passes through the centre.

Classroom Connection

Use paper cutouts, matchsticks, geoboards, floor tiles, rangoli patterns, and classroom objects. Let children sort shapes by number of sides, corners, equal sides, and curved or straight boundaries.

One-minute Revision

  • Plane figures are flat two-dimensional figures.
  • Triangle has 3 sides; quadrilateral has 4 sides.
  • Square is a special rectangle with all sides equal.
  • Circle has centre, radius, diameter, chord, arc, and circumference.
  • Polygons are closed figures made of line segments.

Common Exam Lines

  • Define plane figure and closed figure.
  • Write properties of triangle, rectangle, square, and circle.
  • Differentiate between square and rectangle.
  • Explain how shape concepts can be taught using local classroom materials.
