Decimal Numbers

This chapter revises decimal numbers as another way of writing tenths, hundredths and thousandths, with links to money, measurement and fractions.

Place Value In Decimals

  • Places to the left of the decimal point are ones, tens, hundreds and so on.
  • Places to the right are tenths, hundredths, thousandths and so on.
  • Example: 26.348 = 2 tens + 6 ones + 3 tenths + 4 hundredths + 8 thousandths.
  • Zero at the end of a decimal does not change value: 2.5 = 2.50.

Operations With Decimals

  • For addition and subtraction, write decimal points one below another and align place values.
  • For multiplication by 10, 100 and 1000, digits shift left in value: 3.45 x 10 = 34.5.
  • For division by 10, 100 and 1000, digits shift right in value: 345 ÷ 100 = 3.45.
  • In decimal multiplication, multiply as whole numbers, then place the decimal according to total decimal places.
  • In decimal division, convert divisor to a whole number when needed and adjust dividend similarly.

Comparing And Ordering Decimals

  • Compare whole-number parts first.
  • If whole parts are equal, compare tenths, then hundredths, then thousandths.
  • Use trailing zeroes to make comparison easier: 4.7 = 4.70, so 4.70 is greater than 4.07.
  • Number line activities help children see that 0.9 is greater than 0.75.

Common Errors

  • Children may think 0.45 is greater than 0.5 because 45 is greater than 5. Use place-value charts.
  • They may forget to align decimal points in addition. Use graph paper or place-value columns.
  • They may place the decimal point randomly in multiplication. Use estimation to check.

Classroom Connection

Make a shop activity with price tags such as Rs 7.50, Rs 12.75 and Rs 3.25. Students add bills, compare prices and connect decimals with rupees and paise.

One-minute Revision

  • Decimals extend the base-ten place-value system to parts smaller than one.
  • 0.1, 0.01 and 0.001 mean tenth, hundredth and thousandth.
  • Decimal points must be aligned for addition and subtraction.
  • Fractions with denominators 10, 100 and 1000 convert easily to decimals.
  • Money and measurement are strong contexts for decimal teaching.

Common Exam Lines

  • A decimal number represents whole units and parts of a unit using the base-ten system.
  • Decimal place value should be taught through charts, grids, money and measurement.
  • Adding zeroes to the right of a decimal does not change its value.
