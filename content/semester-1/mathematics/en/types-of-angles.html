Types of Angles

This chapter builds quick recognition of angles by size, position, and use. For exams, remember the degree limits and connect them with objects children see every day.

Exam Snapshot

TermFast meaningExample
AngleFigure formed by two rays with one common endpoint.Two clock hands meeting at the centre.

Main Types by Measurement

TypeMeasureMemory clueClassroom example
Zero angle0 degreesBoth arms overlap.Closed compass arms.
Acute angleMore than 0 and less than 90 degrees.Small and sharp.Slightly opened book.
Right angleExactly 90 degrees.Perfect corner.Notebook corner or window frame.
Obtuse angleMore than 90 and less than 180 degrees.Wider than a right angle.Door opened wide.
Straight angleExactly 180 degrees.Half turn.Opposite rays on one line.
Reflex angleMore than 180 and less than 360 degrees.Larger outside turn.Large turn of a clock hand.
Complete angleExactly 360 degrees.Full turn.One full rotation.

Pairs of Angles

  • Complementary angles: two angles whose sum is 90 degrees. Example: 30 degrees + 60 degrees.
  • Supplementary angles: two angles whose sum is 180 degrees. Example: 110 degrees + 70 degrees.
  • Adjacent angles: two angles sharing a common vertex and one common arm, without overlapping interiors.
  • Linear pair: adjacent angles whose non-common arms form a straight line; their sum is 180 degrees.
  • Vertically opposite angles: angles opposite to each other when two lines intersect; they are equal.

How to Identify Quickly

  1. Mark the vertex first.
  2. Observe the opening between the arms.
  3. Compare the opening with a right angle.
  4. If exact measurement is needed, place the protractor centre at the vertex and read from the correct zero line.
  5. Write the angle name with the vertex letter in the middle, such as angle AOB.

Common Confusions

ConfusionCorrect idea
Longer arms mean bigger angle.Angle size depends on opening, not arm length.
Any wide angle is reflex.Obtuse is between 90 and 180; reflex is above 180.
30 and 70 degrees are complementary.Complementary sum must be exactly 90 degrees.
A straight line has no angle.A straight line forms a straight angle of 180 degrees.

Classroom Connection

Ask children to find angles in the classroom: book corners, door openings, clock hands, scissor blades, and folded paper. A paper right-angle checker helps learners compare acute, right, and obtuse angles before using a protractor.

One-minute Revision

  • Angle = two rays with a common endpoint.
  • Acute: below 90 degrees; right: 90 degrees; obtuse: between 90 and 180 degrees.
  • Straight angle is 180 degrees; complete angle is 360 degrees.
  • Complementary sum is 90 degrees; supplementary sum is 180 degrees.
  • Angle size depends on opening, not on length of arms.

Common Exam Lines

  • Define angle and name its parts.
  • Classify angles according to their measurement.
  • Differentiate between acute, right, obtuse, straight, reflex, and complete angles.
  • Explain complementary and supplementary angles with examples.
