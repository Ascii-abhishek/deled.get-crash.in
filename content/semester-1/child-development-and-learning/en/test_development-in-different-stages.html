Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Name the main areas of development studied stage-wise.

The main areas are physical, mental or cognitive, emotional, language, social, moral, and creative development. These areas influence one another at every stage.

2. Write two features of early childhood.

Early childhood shows rapid language growth, imagination, imitation, play, direct emotional expression, and development of self-help habits. Learning should be play-based and sensory.

3. Why is concrete teaching important in later childhood?

Children in later childhood understand better through concrete examples, objects, maps, experiments, and number games. Concrete experience prepares them for symbols, rules, and abstract ideas.

4. How does emotional development affect learning?

Emotional security increases confidence, participation, attention, and memory. Fear, humiliation, comparison, and failure without support may reduce learning and self-expression.

5. Give two ways to support creative development.

Teachers can support creativity through drawing, models, craft, drama, stories, open-ended questions, local problem-solving tasks, and acceptance of different correct methods.

2. Discuss domain-wise classroom implications of development.
  • Physical: Check health, posture, vision, hearing, motor control, and stamina; include movement and suitable seating.
  • Cognitive: Use concrete material, examples, questions, and experiments before abstract rules.
  • Emotional: Avoid fear and humiliation; give encouragement and predictable routines.
  • Language: Use conversation, reading aloud, vocabulary work, local language support, and writing scaffolds.
  • Social: Provide group work, peer help, rotating roles, and fair class responsibilities.
  • Creative: Encourage drawing, models, drama, open questions, and multiple solutions.

4. What should a teacher do when a child's performance does not match age expectations?

The teacher should observe carefully before judging.

  • Check possible physical factors such as eyesight, hearing, posture, health, and motor readiness.
  • Check cognitive readiness, language expression, emotional security, and home support.
  • Use remedial activities, peer help, concrete material, oral response, and flexible time.
  • Talk respectfully with the child and parents or caregivers.
  • If the difficulty continues, refer the child for appropriate professional or institutional support.
