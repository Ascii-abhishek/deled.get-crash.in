Development in Different Stages

This chapter revises how development appears in different stages of life. The exam focus is not only naming stages, but explaining physical, mental, emotional, language, cognitive, social, and creative changes with classroom implications.

Exam Snapshot

Area What to remember Teacher's link
Physical Growth, motor control, health, coordination, stamina, and sense organs. Plan movement, handwriting practice, seating, games, and rest.
Mental / cognitive Attention, memory, reasoning, concept formation, imagination, and problem solving. Move from concrete examples to symbols and rules.
Emotional Fear, anger, joy, jealousy, confidence, self-control, and security. Create a safe climate and avoid humiliation.
Language Listening, speaking, vocabulary, expression, reading, and writing. Use stories, conversation, questions, reading aloud, and writing practice.
Social Sharing, cooperation, leadership, peer group, rules, and responsibility. Use pair work, group tasks, class duties, and fair rules.
Creative Curiosity, imagination, originality, drawing, craft, drama, and new solutions. Give open-ended tasks and accept more than one correct way.

Stage-wise Overview

Stage Approximate age Dominant needs Best learning support
Infancy Birth to about 2 years Security, nutrition, attachment, sensory experience, movement. Safe surroundings, touch-and-see objects, talk, songs, rhythm, affectionate response.
Early childhood About 2 to 6 years Play, language, imagination, imitation, self-help habits. Story, role play, picture talk, clay, blocks, music, short activities.
Later childhood About 6 to 12 years Peer group, rules, concrete reasoning, school habits, skill practice. Projects, experiments, maps, number games, group work, practice, feedback.
Adolescence About 12 to 18 years Identity, dignity, guidance, emotional balance, future direction. Discussion, debate, counseling attitude, life skills, responsibility, value education.

Infancy: Developmental Pattern

  • Physical: Very rapid growth; control moves from head to trunk to legs. The infant learns to roll, sit, crawl, stand, and walk gradually.
  • Sensory and cognitive: Learning happens through seeing, hearing, touching, tasting, moving, and repeating actions.
  • Emotional: Attachment, trust, fear of strangers, joy, and crying are strong. Warm response builds security.
  • Language: Crying, cooing, babbling, gestures, first words, and simple phrases appear step by step.
  • Social: The infant first relates with parents or caregivers, then responds to familiar people.
  • Creative beginnings: Exploration is the early base of creativity: touching, shaking, dropping, and imitating.

Early Childhood: Developmental Pattern

  • Physical: Gross motor skills improve: running, jumping, balancing, throwing, climbing. Fine motor control develops through drawing, clay, beads, and blocks.
  • Mental and cognitive: Thinking is imaginative and concrete. The child asks many questions and learns through play and direct experience.
  • Emotional: Emotions are direct and change quickly. Fear, anger, possessiveness, jealousy, affection, and joy are common.
  • Language: Vocabulary grows quickly. The child enjoys stories, rhymes, conversation, and naming objects.
  • Social: Parallel play slowly becomes cooperative play. Imitation of adults is strong.
  • Creative: Drawing, pretend play, singing, rhythm, craft, and storytelling help self-expression.

Later Childhood: Developmental Pattern

  • Physical: Growth becomes steadier. Coordination improves, making games, handwriting, craft, and school routines easier.
  • Mental and cognitive: The child begins to reason logically with concrete examples. Classification, conservation, number ideas, maps, and cause-effect thinking improve.
  • Emotional: Self-control increases, but children still need appreciation and security. Failure, comparison, and ridicule can reduce confidence.
  • Language: Reading, writing, narration, question-answer, vocabulary, grammar, and listening comprehension develop quickly.
  • Social: Peer group becomes important. Children learn rules, cooperation, fairness, leadership, and responsibility.
  • Creative: Projects, models, drawing, local problem-solving, dramatization, poems, and stories support originality.

Adolescence: Developmental Pattern

  • Physical: Puberty brings rapid height, weight, voice, body proportion, and reproductive maturity changes.
  • Mental and cognitive: Abstract thinking, reasoning about possibilities, ideals, social issues, and future planning develop.
  • Emotional: Emotions can be intense. Self-respect, privacy, belonging, and identity become important.
  • Language: Debate, argument, self-expression, literature, critical reading, and formal writing improve with practice.
  • Social: Peer approval is strong. The adolescent forms ideals, friendships, group identity, and social opinions.
  • Creative: Music, writing, art, science models, social projects, and problem-solving can become serious interests.

Domain-wise Classroom Implications

Development area If ignored Teacher's response
Physical Child may be labelled lazy or careless when the real issue is health, vision, hearing, posture, or motor control. Observe, adjust seating, include movement, use large-to-small writing practice, and refer when needed.
Cognitive Abstract teaching may create rote learning and fear. Use concrete objects, examples, questions, experiments, and step-by-step problem solving.
Emotional Fear and humiliation reduce participation and memory. Give encouragement, predictable routines, respectful correction, and chances to succeed.
Language Children may know the idea but fail to express it. Use oral work, local language support, vocabulary building, reading aloud, and writing scaffolds.
Social Isolation, conflict, or unfair competition can disturb learning. Use group norms, rotating roles, peer help, games, and class responsibilities.
Creative Only copying may reduce curiosity and originality. Ask open questions, accept different methods, use drawing/model/drama, and value original effort.

Important Differences

Early childhood Later childhood
Play, imagination, imitation, and rapid language growth dominate. Rules, school habits, peer group, and concrete reasoning dominate.
Activities should be short, sensory, rhythmic, and story-based. Activities can include projects, experiments, worksheets, maps, and group discussion.
Self-control is limited and emotions change quickly. Self-control improves but encouragement and fair discipline are still needed.

Classroom Situations

  • If a Class 1 child reverses letters, check visual-motor coordination and writing readiness before calling it carelessness.
  • If a Class 3 child gives the answer orally but cannot write it, strengthen language and writing support.
  • If a child avoids group work, observe social confidence, peer relations, and emotional security.
  • If a child keeps asking "why", use it as cognitive curiosity, not as disturbance.
  • If an adolescent argues, channel it into debate, evidence-based discussion, and respectful dialogue.

Quick Diagram

Early childhood  -> play + language + imagination
Later childhood  -> concrete thinking + peers + school habits
Adolescence      -> identity + abstract thought + guidance

Across all stages: body, mind, emotion, language, society, creativity grow together.

One-minute Revision

  • Development must be studied area-wise and stage-wise.
  • Physical, cognitive, emotional, language, social, and creative development influence one another.
  • Infancy needs security and sensory experience; early childhood needs play and language.
  • Later childhood needs concrete learning, practice, group work, and fair rules.
  • Adolescence needs guidance, respect, responsibility, and emotional support.
  • A teacher should observe before judging and support before punishing.

Common Exam Lines

  • Development in one area influences development in all other areas.
  • Play is the natural medium of learning in childhood.
  • Concrete experiences are necessary before abstract ideas in primary classes.
  • Emotional security is the foundation of confident learning.
  • Developmental understanding helps the teacher provide age-appropriate and inclusive learning experiences.
