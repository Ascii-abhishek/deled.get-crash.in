Meaning and Theories of Learning

This chapter revises learning as a change in behavior, knowledge, skill, attitude, and understanding through experience. For D.El.Ed exams, write theories with classroom use, not as isolated names.

Exam Snapshot

Topic Quick revision Classroom link
Meaning of learning A relatively permanent change caused by experience, practice, interaction, and reflection. A child learns subtraction by using objects, discussion, practice, and feedback.
Nature of learning Continuous, purposeful, active, social, individual, and influenced by readiness. Children learn better when the task is meaningful and suited to their level.

Meaning And Nature Of Learning

  • Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior or capacity. Temporary fatigue, illness, or mood change is not learning.
  • Learning may be visible, such as reading aloud, writing neatly, solving sums, or following classroom rules.
  • Learning may also be internal, such as understanding a concept, changing an attitude, developing interest, or forming a value.
  • Learning is active. The learner observes, imitates, asks, tries, fails, corrects, practices, and reflects.
  • Learning is individual as well as social. A child learns at a personal pace but also learns through teacher guidance, peer interaction, language, and culture.

Characteristics Of Learning

Characteristic Meaning Teacher's action
Continuous Learning continues from birth through life. Connect school learning with home and community experience.
Purposeful Learning improves when the learner sees a need or goal. State the purpose in simple child-friendly language.
Active Children learn by doing, thinking, speaking, and correcting. Use activities, experiments, questions, and practice.
Result of experience Direct and indirect experiences both shape learning. Use local examples, stories, visits, models, and demonstrations.
Individual differences Children differ in pace, readiness, interest, and background. Use flexible grouping, remedial support, and enrichment.
Relatively permanent Learning lasts longer than temporary behavior changes. Use revision, application, and feedback to strengthen learning.

Factors Affecting Learning

Factor Effect on learning Teacher response
Readiness If the child is not mentally or physically ready, learning becomes mechanical. Check prior knowledge and begin with simple concrete activity.
Maturity Certain skills require age and developmental preparation. Do not force abstract tasks before the child is ready.
Motivation and interest Interest increases attention, effort, and persistence. Use stories, games, praise, choice, and real-life examples.
Health and nutrition Illness, hunger, fatigue, or sensory difficulty reduces attention. Observe physical needs and refer when necessary.
Attention Without attention, experience does not become learning. Use variety, questions, movement, and clear instructions.
Previous knowledge Old learning may help or hinder new learning. Link new lesson with familiar examples.
Method and material Unsuitable methods create rote learning and fear. Use activity, demonstration, TLM, peer work, and feedback.
Classroom environment Fear, ridicule, noise, and unfairness reduce learning. Create safety, respect, routine, and participation.

Effective Methods Of Learning

  • Learning by doing: Children understand better when they handle objects, measure, draw, make, sort, count, and experiment.
  • Imitation: Young children learn language, manners, pronunciation, handwriting posture, and classroom routines by copying adults and peers.
  • Observation: Watching a plant grow, a magnet attract, a map being read, or a teacher solving a sum builds concepts.
  • Trial and error: A learner tries different responses, removes wrong attempts, and keeps the successful response.
  • Practice and repetition: Repeated work strengthens skill when it includes meaning, feedback, and correction.
  • Group learning: Discussion, peer help, games, and cooperative work improve expression and social learning.
  • Project and problem-solving: Children connect knowledge with real tasks such as making a weather chart or solving a water-use problem.
  • Insight: The child suddenly understands the relation among parts after studying the whole problem.

Thorndike's Laws Of Learning

Law Meaning Primary classroom use
Law of readiness When the learner is ready to learn, learning gives satisfaction; when not ready, forced learning causes annoyance. Prepare interest, connect previous knowledge, and start from the child's level.
Law of exercise Practice strengthens connections; lack of practice weakens them. Give meaningful repetition, oral practice, written practice, and revision.
Law of effect Responses followed by satisfaction are strengthened; responses followed by discomfort are weakened. Use encouragement, success experience, feedback, and joyful practice.
Law of multiple response The learner tries many responses until a useful one is found. Allow mistakes and guide correction.
Law of analogy Past similar experiences help solve new situations. Use examples from known lessons and daily life.

Major Theories Of Learning

Theory / psychologist Main idea Classroom use
Thorndike: trial and error Learning occurs by trying responses, removing errors, and retaining successful responses. Give practice, feedback, small steps, and chances to correct mistakes.
Pavlov: classical conditioning Learning happens through association between stimulus and response. Create positive associations with school, reading, mathematics, and participation.
Skinner: operant conditioning Behavior is shaped by reinforcement and consequences. Use timely praise, tokens, immediate feedback, and reinforcement of desired behavior.
Kohler: insight learning Learning can occur suddenly when the learner understands the whole problem situation. Give puzzles, problem situations, manipulatives, and time to think.
Piaget: cognitive constructivism Children construct knowledge according to their cognitive stage through activity and experience. Use concrete material, discovery, classification, conservation, and stage-appropriate tasks.
Vygotsky: social constructivism Learning grows through language, social interaction, and support within the zone of proximal development. Use scaffolding, peer work, guided questions, dialogue, and gradual withdrawal of help.
Bandura: observational learning Children learn by observing models and the consequences of behavior. Model respectful speech, reading habits, problem-solving, and cooperation.

Behaviorist, Cognitive, And Social Views

View Focus Useful when Caution
Behaviorist Observable behavior, practice, reinforcement, stimulus-response. Building habits, skills, routines, pronunciation, tables, and discipline. Do not reduce learning to reward and punishment only.
Cognitive Understanding, thinking, memory, insight, stages, and concept formation. Teaching concepts, reasoning, problem-solving, and understanding relationships. Do not give abstract tasks without concrete base.
Social constructivist Language, interaction, culture, scaffolding, and peer learning. Discussion, group work, guided reading, collaborative problem-solving. Group work still needs clear roles and teacher support.

Transfer Of Learning

  • Positive transfer: Previous learning helps new learning. Example: counting helps addition; map reading helps globe study.
  • Negative transfer: Old habit interferes with new learning. Example: mother tongue sound patterns may affect English pronunciation.
  • Zero transfer: Previous learning has little or no effect on the new task.
  • Teacher use: Connect related lessons, point out similarities and differences, and correct wrong transfer early.

Learning Curve And Plateau

A learning curve shows progress during practice. Sometimes progress becomes fast, sometimes slow, and sometimes it appears to stop for a short time. This temporary stop is called a learning plateau.

  • Causes of plateau: fatigue, boredom, poor method, lack of feedback, low motivation, task difficulty, or old habit.
  • Remedies: give rest, change method, break task into steps, provide feedback, use peer support, and rebuild interest.
  • Exam line: Plateau does not mean learning has ended; it means the learner needs better method, rest, or motivation.

Classroom Connection

When children learn multiplication tables only by repetition, some may remember but not understand. A better teacher combines practice with grouping objects, skip counting, story problems, peer recitation, quick feedback, and small reinforcement. This uses behaviorist practice, cognitive understanding, and social learning together.

One-minute Revision

  • Learning is more than memorization; it includes change in understanding, skill, attitude, and behavior.
  • Learning is active, continuous, purposeful, social, and affected by individual differences.
  • Readiness, motivation, health, attention, previous knowledge, method, and environment affect learning.
  • Thorndike's readiness, exercise, and effect are key laws of learning.
  • Thorndike, Pavlov, Skinner, Kohler, Piaget, Vygotsky, and Bandura are commonly asked.
  • Primary teaching should move from concrete experience to symbols, rules, and application.
  • Practice with feedback is better than blind repetition.

Common Exam Lines

  • Learning is a continuous and purposeful process of relatively permanent change.
  • Learning becomes effective when the learner is ready, interested, active, and supported.
  • The teacher should combine behaviorist, cognitive, and social approaches according to the classroom need.
  • Reinforcement should encourage desirable learning, not create fear or dependency.
  • Concrete experience, language, interaction, and feedback are essential in primary classrooms.
  • A learning plateau can be removed by rest, method change, feedback, and motivation.
