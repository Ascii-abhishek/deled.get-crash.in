Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define motivation.

Motivation is the inner or outer force that arouses, directs, and maintains behavior toward a goal. In learning it makes the child ready to listen, try, practice, and complete work.

2. What is attention?

Attention is the selective focusing of consciousness on a particular object, idea, person, or activity. Learning needs attention because the mind cannot focus equally on everything at once.

3. Write two types of motivation.

Intrinsic motivation comes from curiosity, joy, mastery, or personal meaning. Extrinsic motivation comes from praise, marks, certificates, rewards, approval, or competition.

4. Mention any four factors affecting attention.

Attention is affected by novelty, size, color, movement, contrast, intensity, interest, need, health, fatigue, emotion, readiness, and past experience.

5. How is interest useful in learning?

Interest makes the learner willingly attend to and engage with an activity. It makes learning pleasant, meaningful, and more self-sustaining.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Explain motivation and its types with classroom examples.

Motivation arouses, directs, and maintains behavior toward a goal. It is essential for effort, persistence, and active participation.

  • Intrinsic: learning due to curiosity or joy, such as reading stories for pleasure.
  • Extrinsic: learning due to praise, marks, certificates, or rewards.
  • Achievement: desire to improve and complete challenging tasks.
  • Social: desire for cooperation, belonging, approval, and responsibility.

Teachers can develop motivation through clear goals, success experiences, life-related examples, praise for effort, feedback, and activity choices.

2. Explain attention, its types, and factors.

Attention is selective focusing of consciousness. It is limited and can shift easily.

  • Voluntary attention: intentional focus due to purpose, such as listening to experiment instructions.
  • Involuntary attention: sudden attention due to bright, loud, moving, or surprising stimulus.
  • Habitual attention: attention formed through habit or interest.
  • Object factors: novelty, size, color, movement, contrast, intensity, repetition.
  • Learner factors: need, interest, motive, health, fatigue, emotion, readiness, past experience.
3. What is interest? How can a teacher develop it?

Interest is a positive feeling or tendency that makes the learner willingly engage with a subject or activity.

  • Use children's existing interests such as games, stories, local events, and environment.
  • Give success experiences and avoid repeated failure.
  • Use experiments, models, drawing, drama, survey, project, and games.
  • Connect learning with real life, such as measuring, reading signs, or making charts.
  • Use observation, conversation, choice, and interest inventories to understand interests.
4. Discuss the relationship among motivation, attention, and interest.

Motivation, attention, and interest work together in learning.

  • Motivation answers "Why should I learn?" and starts effort.
  • Attention answers "What should I focus on?" and directs mental energy.
  • Interest answers "Why do I want to stay with this?" and sustains engagement.
  • Interest supports attention; attention supports effective learning; success and feedback increase motivation.
  • A good lesson creates curiosity, gives a clear task, uses activity, provides feedback, and builds success.
5. Suggest ways to remove barriers to motivation, attention, and interest.
  • Replace fear and humiliation with respectful correction and effort-based praise.
  • Reduce monotony by changing method, material, grouping, and question type.
  • Use graded tasks so work is neither too easy nor too difficult.
  • Observe fatigue, hunger, illness, and sensory problems before judging behavior.
  • Connect content with life, local examples, projects, stories, and real problems.
  • Give clear instructions, short steps, activity, feedback, and success experiences.
