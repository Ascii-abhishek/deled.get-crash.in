Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define child development.

Child development is the study of orderly and continuous changes in a child from birth or conception to maturity. It includes physical, cognitive, language, emotional, social, moral, and creative changes.

3. Why is the study of child development useful for teachers?

It helps teachers understand readiness, age level, individual differences, interests, emotional needs, and suitable teaching methods. It supports child-centered teaching, inclusive education, positive discipline, and better assessment.

4. Name any four areas of child development.

Physical development, cognitive or mental development, language development, emotional development, social development, moral development, and creative development are major areas. Any four of these may be written.

5. What is meant by heredity and environment in child development?

Heredity means biological potential received from parents. Environment means the surroundings and experiences that influence development, such as family, nutrition, school, peer group, language exposure, culture, and learning opportunities.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the meaning, need, and scope of child development.

Meaning: Child development is the continuous and progressive change in a child from birth/conception to maturity.

Need:

  • It helps the teacher understand age level, readiness, and learning capacity.
  • It supports child-centered and activity-based teaching.
  • It helps identify individual differences, learning difficulties, and special needs.
  • It improves discipline, guidance, and assessment.

Scope: It includes physical, cognitive, language, emotional, social, moral, and creative development, along with the influence of heredity, environment, family, school, peers, and culture.

2. Describe the major principles of child development with examples.
  • Continuity: Development begins before birth and continues in an organized way.
  • Sequence: Children usually sit before standing and speak words before sentences.
  • General to specific: Whole-hand movement comes before fine finger control.
  • Head to foot: Head and neck control develop before walking.
  • Individual pace: Children of the same age may differ in language, confidence, and motor skill.
  • Interrelation: Health, emotion, language, and learning affect one another.
  • Heredity and environment: Potential comes through heredity, but opportunity comes through environment.

4. How should a primary teacher use knowledge of child development in the classroom?

A primary teacher should observe learners and plan instruction according to developmental level.

  • Begin with concrete objects, local examples, play, stories, pictures, and action.
  • Provide varied opportunities: speaking, drawing, group work, writing, and hands-on tasks.
  • Respect individual differences and compare the child with the child's own progress.
  • Use positive discipline through routines, explanation, responsibility, and encouragement.
  • Support language, emotion, social cooperation, creativity, and moral behavior along with subject learning.
  • Identify delays or difficulties early and provide remedial or referral support.
