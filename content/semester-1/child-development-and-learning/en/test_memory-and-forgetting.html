Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define memory.

Memory is the mental power or process by which learned experience is retained and later recalled or recognized when needed.

2. Name the main steps of memory.

The main steps are learning, retention, recall, and recognition. In modern terms these are related to encoding, storage, and retrieval.

3. Mention four factors affecting memory.

Meaningfulness, attention, interest, motivation, repetition, association, organization, health, emotion, and practice method affect memory.

4. What is forgetting?

Forgetting is failure or difficulty in recalling or recognizing previously learned material. It may occur due to disuse, interference, poor understanding, anxiety, fatigue, or missing cues.

5. Write two ways to improve memory.

Memory can be improved through meaningful learning, spaced repetition, active recall, association, grouping, diagrams, feedback, and application of learned material.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Explain the meaning, process, and types of memory.

Memory is the retention and later recall or recognition of learned experience.

  • Process: learning, retention, recall, recognition; or encoding, storage, retrieval.
  • Sensory memory: very brief memory of sensory experience.
  • Short-term memory: limited information held briefly.
  • Long-term memory: knowledge, skills, and experiences stored for longer periods.
  • Rote memory: memory by repetition.
  • Logical memory: memory through meaning, relation, and understanding.
2. Discuss factors affecting memory and their educational implications.
  • Meaning: understood material is remembered better; use examples and diagrams.
  • Attention: without attention, encoding is weak; use activity and clear instructions.
  • Interest and motivation: life-related lessons receive better effort.
  • Repetition: spaced revision strengthens retention.
  • Association: linking new ideas with old knowledge improves recall.
  • Organization: headings, tables, and charts help retrieval.
  • Health and emotion: fatigue, anxiety, and fear reduce memory.
3. Explain forgetting, its causes, and theories.

Forgetting is the failure to recall or recognize previously learned material.

  • Disuse: lack of practice weakens memory.
  • Interference: one learning disturbs another.
  • Poor understanding: meaningless rote material is forgotten quickly.
  • Emotional factors: anxiety, fear, and stress block recall.
  • Decay theory: memory traces weaken over time if unused.
  • Retrieval cue theory: material is stored but cues are missing.
4. Suggest classroom methods to improve memory and reduce forgetting.
  • Teach meaning before memorization.
  • Use diagrams, charts, examples, stories, and local links.
  • Use spaced revision and active recall.
  • Connect new material with previous knowledge.
  • Organize material into headings, categories, and steps.
  • Use multisensory methods: see, hear, speak, write, draw, act, and do.
  • Give feedback and application-based practice.
  • Reduce fear and test anxiety.
