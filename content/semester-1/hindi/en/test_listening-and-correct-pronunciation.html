Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define Listening and Correct Pronunciation in the context of primary teaching.

Listening and Correct Pronunciation means the classroom knowledge and practice through which children understand, use, and revise this part of language learning. It should be linked with examples, oral work, reading, writing, and feedback.

2. Write any three classroom activities for this chapter.

Useful activities include picture/word matching, oral repetition, pair practice, blackboard modelling, fill in blanks, correction of errors, and short notebook work based on familiar examples.

3. Mention two common learner errors.

Common errors include confusing similar sounds or forms, memorising without meaning, missing marks/endings, and using a pattern in the wrong context. The teacher should diagnose the exact error before correction.

4. How should a teacher correct mistakes in this topic?

Correction should be gentle, immediate where needed, and example-based. The teacher should give the correct model, let the child repeat or rewrite, and then use the form in a meaningful sentence.

5. Why is practice more useful than lecture here?

Language skills become strong by use. Practice lets children listen, speak, read, write, compare, and self-correct; lecture only gives information and does not create fluency.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the importance of Listening and Correct Pronunciation for a primary teacher.

A primary teacher needs Listening and Correct Pronunciation because it supports clear language learning, meaningful classroom interaction, and confident participation. The teacher must know the concept, present it through simple examples, connect it with children's surroundings, and assess it through oral and written tasks. Its importance lies in building accuracy, fluency, vocabulary, comprehension, and independent expression. A good teacher moves from concrete examples to practice and then to short revision.

2. Describe a step-by-step teaching plan for this chapter.

Step 1: start with a familiar example or oral question. Step 2: present two or three clear examples on the board. Step 3: explain the rule or pattern briefly. Step 4: conduct guided practice with the whole class. Step 5: give pair or group activity. Step 6: ask children to complete a notebook or oral task. Step 7: give feedback and close with one-minute revision.

3. Write common errors, causes, and remedies related to this topic.

Errors usually arise from weak listening, unclear examples, over-memorisation, lack of practice, and confusion between similar forms. Remedies include modelling, comparison tables, picture support, repeated oral practice, short written drills, peer correction, and respectful teacher feedback. The teacher should correct the pattern, not the child personally.

4. How will you evaluate learning in this topic?

Evaluation should be continuous and activity-based. Use oral questions, matching tasks, fill in blanks, reading aloud, short writing, error correction, sentence making, and observation checklist. Good evaluation checks understanding, accuracy, expression, and transfer to new examples, not only memorised definitions.
