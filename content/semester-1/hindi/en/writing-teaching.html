Writing Teaching

This chapter explains how primary teachers develop handwriting, spelling, copying, dictation, sentence writing, and independent expression.

Core Ideas

  • Writing teaching begins before letters: posture, grip, hand-eye coordination, direction, spacing, and fine motor control.
  • Children need correct sitting position, notebook angle, pencil grip, and adequate light.
  • Letter formation should follow correct stroke direction and proportion, not merely final shape.
  • Copywriting improves neatness; dictation checks sound-letter relation, spelling, listening, and memory.
  • Writing develops from strokes to letters, letters to words, words to sentences, and sentences to paragraphs.
  • Teachers should use blackboard modelling, tracing, pattern practice, word cards, picture-based sentences, and peer reading.
  • Common errors include mirror writing, missing matras, wrong spacing, incomplete shirorekha, and incorrect letter size.
  • Correction should be selective: mark key errors and ask for short re-practice instead of red-inking every line.
  • Creative writing begins with oral discussion, picture talk, sentence frames, and familiar topics.
  • Assessment should value legibility, spelling, punctuation, sequence, expression, and completion.

Classroom Connection

  • Begin with a two-minute oral warm-up connected to the child's home and school experience.
  • Model once, practise together, then let pairs practise while the teacher listens and supports.
  • Use blackboard, cards, pictures, real objects, short poems, and quick games instead of long lecture.
  • Keep correction respectful: show the correct pattern and ask the child to try again in a meaningful sentence.
  • End with a tiny exit task: one word, one sentence, one oral response, or one corrected example.

Quick Teaching Flow

Warm-up -> Model -> Guided practice -> Pair work -> Notebook/oral task -> Feedback
