Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define rural area.

A rural area is a village or countryside settlement, usually with lower population density and close links with land and local resources.

2. Define urban area.

An urban area is a town or city settlement with higher population density, built-up spaces, and many services.

3. Give two rural occupations.

Farming, agricultural labour, animal rearing, craft work, fishing, and local shopkeeping are rural occupations. Any two may be written.

4. Give two urban occupations.

Factory work, shop work, transport, office work, construction, and professional services are urban occupations. Any two may be written.

5. What is rural-urban interdependence?

It means villages and cities depend on one another for food, markets, labour, services, education, health care, and transport.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Differentiate between rural and urban lifestyle.

Rural lifestyle usually has lower population density, more open space, close community ties, and livelihoods linked with agriculture, animals, crafts, and local services. Urban lifestyle has higher density, more built-up areas, diverse people, and livelihoods linked with industry, trade, offices, transport, and services.

2. Explain rural-urban interdependence.
  • Villages supply food, milk, labour, and raw materials.
  • Cities supply markets, hospitals, colleges, machinery, and administrative services.
  • Migration sends income and ideas between both places.
  • Roads, phones, banks, and internet strengthen the link.
3. Write major problems of rural and urban areas.

Rural areas may face seasonal employment, irrigation gaps, debt, weak health facilities, and limited transport. Urban areas may face crowding, pollution, traffic, slums, high living costs, and unequal access to services. Both need planned development and dignity for all workers.

4. How can a teacher teach this topic sensitively?
  • Use examples from children's own communities.
  • Avoid calling any lifestyle superior or inferior.
  • Compare needs, occupations, services, and problems fairly.
  • Use charts, maps, interviews, and picture cards.
  • Discuss shared needs such as water, school, health, safety, and respect.
