Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define history.

History is the evidence-based study of past human life, events, societies, and changes.

2. What are historical sources?

Historical sources are evidences such as coins, inscriptions, texts, tools, buildings, photographs, and oral memories that help us know the past.

3. Give two examples of archaeological sources.

Coins and pottery are archaeological sources. Tools, monuments, seals, and bones are also examples.

4. What is the difference between primary and secondary sources?

A primary source belongs to the period being studied. A secondary source is a later explanation based on primary sources.

5. Why is history important for citizenship?

History helps learners understand rights, duties, democratic values, national movements, and lessons from past social change.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the meaning and importance of history.
  • History is the study of human past through evidence.
  • It explains continuity and change in society.
  • It builds cultural and national awareness.
  • It develops critical thinking by asking evidence-based questions.
  • It promotes respect for diversity and responsible citizenship.
2. Classify the sources of history with examples.
  • Archaeological: tools, pottery, coins, monuments, bones.
  • Literary: manuscripts, books, religious texts, biographies.
  • Inscriptions: writings on stone, metal, walls, or pillars.
  • Oral: folk songs, legends, interviews, family memories.
  • Visual: maps, paintings, photographs, films.
  • Official records: census, reports, laws, court documents.
3. Discuss the limitations of historical sources.

Sources may be damaged, incomplete, biased, or difficult to date. Written sources may represent only rulers or powerful groups. Oral sources can change over time. Therefore historians compare different sources before reaching a conclusion.

4. How will you teach sources of history in primary classes?
  • Create a history corner with old coins, photos, tools, and local objects.
  • Ask children to interview elders about past school, festivals, or village life.
  • Use timelines for family and local events.
  • Compare what different sources can tell us.
  • Visit or show pictures of local monuments, museums, and maps.
