Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What is urban life?

Urban life is life in towns and cities where population density is high and people work mainly in industry, trade, services, and administration.

2. Name four civic amenities.

Water supply, sanitation, roads, streetlights, waste collection, public health, parks, and public transport are civic amenities. Any four may be written.

3. What is the role of municipality?

Municipality manages urban services such as water, sanitation, roads, lighting, health, records, and local regulation.

4. Mention two problems of urban life.

Housing pressure, traffic congestion, pollution, waste management, inequality, and safety concerns are urban problems. Any two may be written.

5. Why should teachers use local city examples?

Local examples help children connect textbook ideas with real roads, services, markets, workers, and safety issues around them.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Describe the main features of urban life.

Urban life has dense population, diverse occupations, cultural diversity, developed services, markets, transport, and administrative institutions. People work in industry, trade, services, informal work, and government offices. City life gives opportunities but also creates problems such as pollution, congestion, expensive housing, and inequality.

2. Explain the functions of municipality.
  • Provides water supply and drainage.
  • Maintains roads, streetlights, parks, and public spaces.
  • Collects waste and supports sanitation.
  • Supports public health and disease prevention.
  • Maintains local records and regulates markets or buildings.
3. Differentiate between rural and urban life.

Rural life is usually linked with agriculture, lower population density, close community relations, and limited services. Urban life is linked with industry, trade, services, high population density, diversity, and more civic amenities, though access may be unequal.

4. How can a teacher teach urban life in primary classes?

A teacher can use neighbourhood walks, civic-services charts, local worker interviews, traffic-safety discussions, maps of markets and public places, and respectful discussion of all occupations. This builds citizenship and observation skills.
