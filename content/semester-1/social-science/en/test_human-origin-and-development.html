Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What is human evolution?

Human evolution is the gradual biological development of humans from earlier human-like forms over a very long time.

2. Name three sources for studying early humans.

Fossils, stone tools, cave paintings, bones, and settlement remains help us study early humans.

3. What were microliths?

Microliths were small sharp stone tools often fitted into handles, arrows, or spears during the Mesolithic period.

4. Why was fire important?

Fire gave warmth, protection from animals, light, and cooked food, making life safer and more flexible.

5. What change did agriculture bring?

Agriculture changed humans from food gatherers to food producers and encouraged settled village life.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Describe the main stages of early human development.
  • Food gathering from plants, roots, seeds, and fruits.
  • Hunting animals through group cooperation.
  • Making tools for cutting, scraping, digging, and hunting.
  • Using fire for warmth, safety, light, and cooking.
  • Developing language, art, clothing, and shelters.
  • Starting farming, herding, and settled village life.
2. Differentiate between Palaeolithic, Mesolithic, and Neolithic periods.

Palaeolithic age had large rough stone tools and nomadic hunting-gathering life. Mesolithic age had small microliths, improved hunting, fishing, and gradual climate change. Neolithic age had polished tools, farming, domestication, pottery, and permanent settlements.

3. Explain the importance of tools, fire, and language.
  • Tools helped in cutting, digging, hunting, and protection.
  • Fire helped with warmth, cooked food, light, and safety.
  • Language improved communication, planning, memory, and teaching.
  • Together these developments made human groups more cooperative and adaptive.
4. How will you teach early human life to primary children?
  • Use timelines, picture cards, and simple maps.
  • Compare "then and now" for food, house, clothes, tools, and travel.
  • Use clay models of tools and role play of hunting, gathering, and farming.
  • Show cave art images and ask children to interpret them.
  • Connect the lesson with local museums or heritage sites.
