Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What is a globe?

A globe is a three-dimensional small model of the earth that shows its shape, poles, oceans, continents, and directions.

2. Define latitude.

Latitude is an imaginary east-west circle parallel to the Equator, measured north or south of it.

3. Define longitude.

Longitude is an imaginary north-south semicircle joining both poles, measured east or west of the Prime Meridian.

4. Name four important parallels.

Tropic of Cancer, Tropic of Capricorn, Arctic Circle, and Antarctic Circle are important parallels; the Equator is the main reference parallel.

5. Why is the Standard Meridian needed?

A standard meridian gives one uniform time for a country or region, avoiding confusion caused by different local times.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the merits and limitations of a globe.

A globe shows the earth's shape, poles, hemispheres, continents, oceans, rotation, and directions correctly. Its limitation is that small details cannot be shown clearly, it is less portable than a map, and only one side can be seen at a time.

2. Differentiate between latitude and longitude.

Latitudes are parallel east-west circles measured from the Equator. Longitudes are north-south semicircles measured from the Prime Meridian and meeting at the poles. Latitudes help explain heat zones; longitudes help calculate time.

3. Explain how longitudes help in time calculation.

Earth rotates 360 degrees in 24 hours, so it rotates 15 degrees in one hour. Places east of Greenwich are ahead in time and places west are behind. Standard meridians are used to keep uniform time within a country.

4. How will you teach latitude and longitude in class?
  • Use a globe, ball, and threads.
  • Mark Equator, poles, and Prime Meridian.
  • Ask children to locate India.
  • Use grid practice with simple coordinates.
  • Connect longitudes with local and standard time.
