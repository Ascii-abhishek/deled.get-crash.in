Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define celestial body.

A celestial body is a natural object found in space, such as a star, planet, satellite, asteroid, or comet.

2. What is a galaxy?

A galaxy is a huge group of stars, gas, dust, and other celestial bodies. Our galaxy is the Milky Way.

3. Name the eight planets in order.

Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.

4. Differentiate between star and planet.

A star has its own light and heat. A planet does not have its own light and revolves around a star.

5. What is a satellite?

A satellite is a body that revolves around a planet. The Moon is Earth's natural satellite.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain celestial organization from universe to planets.

The universe contains galaxies. A galaxy contains stars, gas, and dust. Our galaxy includes the solar system. The solar system has the Sun at the centre, eight planets, satellites, asteroids, comets, and meteoroids. Earth is one planet in this system.

2. Describe the solar system briefly.
  • The Sun is the central star.
  • Eight planets revolve around the Sun.
  • Planets may have satellites.
  • Earth supports life because it has air, water, and suitable temperature.
  • Asteroids, comets, and meteoroids are also part of the solar system.
3. Differentiate between rotation and revolution.

Rotation is the spinning of a body on its own axis. Revolution is the movement of a body around another body in a fixed path. Earth's rotation causes day and night, while its revolution around the Sun helps form the year and seasonal pattern.

4. How will you teach celestial organization with a classroom model?
  • Use balls of different sizes for the Sun, planets, and Moon.
  • Draw orbit circles with chalk or string.
  • Let children show rotation and revolution physically.
  • Use name cards for planets in correct order.
  • Connect the model with sky observation and calendar events.
