Globe, Latitudes, and Longitudes

A globe is the best small model of the earth. Latitudes and longitudes are imaginary lines used to locate places accurately. For exam preparation, remember important lines, hemispheres, grid, and the link between longitude and time.

Globe: Merits and Limits

  • It shows the earth's shape, oceans, continents, poles, and directions more correctly than a flat map.
  • It helps children understand rotation, day-night, hemispheres, and location.
  • Its limit is that small places cannot be shown in detail and it is not easy to carry like a map.

Longitudes and Time

  • The Prime Meridian is 0 degree longitude and passes through Greenwich.
  • Longitudes are measured 0 degree to 180 degree east and west.
  • Earth rotates 360 degrees in 24 hours, so 15 degrees longitude equals about 1 hour.
  • India uses 82.5 degree E as its Standard Meridian; Indian Standard Time is 5 hours 30 minutes ahead of Greenwich Mean Time.

Grid and Location

When latitude and longitude cross each other, they form a grid. A place is located by writing latitude first and longitude second, such as 28 degree N, 77 degree E.

Classroom Connection

Use a ball and thread to show the equator, poles, and meridians. Let children locate India, then ask them to find whether it lies north or south of the equator and east or west of the Prime Meridian.

One-minute Revision

  • Globe is a small model of the earth.
  • Equator is 0 degree latitude; Prime Meridian is 0 degree longitude.
  • Latitudes are parallel circles; longitudes meet at poles.
  • Latitudes help explain heat zones; longitudes help calculate time.
  • India's Standard Meridian is 82.5 degree E.

Common Exam Lines

  • What is a globe? Write its merits and limitations.
  • Differentiate between latitude and longitude.
  • Name important parallels of latitude.
  • Explain how longitudes help in calculating time.
