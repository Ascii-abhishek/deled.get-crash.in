Mapping

Mapping is the skill of showing places, directions, distances, and features of the earth on a flat surface. For D.El.Ed revision, focus on map types, symbols, scale, direction, and how children can learn maps through local observation.

Why Maps Are Needed

  • Maps help us locate places quickly.
  • They show directions, distances, boundaries, routes, rivers, mountains, roads, and settlements.
  • They compress a large area into a small readable form.
  • They help in travel, administration, disaster management, classroom teaching, and planning.

Essentials of Map Reading

  • Direction: North is usually shown at the top. The four cardinal directions are north, south, east, and west.
  • Scale: It tells how much the real world has been reduced.
  • Symbols: Conventional signs save space and make maps readable.
  • Colours: Blue often shows water, green shows plains or vegetation, brown shows highlands, and yellow may show plateaus or deserts depending on the legend.
  • Legend or key: It explains symbols and colours used in the map.

Classroom Connection

Begin with the child's own space: draw the classroom plan, mark door, windows, blackboard, and desks, then move to school route, village or ward map, district map, and India map. Ask learners to use a key, direction arrow, and simple scale.

One-minute Revision

  • A map is a flat representation of the earth or its part.
  • Three common map types are physical, political, and thematic.
  • Direction, scale, symbols, colours, and legend are basic elements.
  • A sketch is rough; a plan shows a small area in detail.
  • Map teaching should move from local and concrete to wider and abstract.

Common Exam Lines
