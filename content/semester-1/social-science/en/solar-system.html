Solar System

The solar system includes the Sun, eight planets, their satellites, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, meteoroids, and dust. For D.El.Ed preparation, focus on order of planets, basic features, motions, and simple ways to teach space concepts.

Order of Planets

From the Sun outward: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.

Sun - Mercury - Venus - Earth - Mars - Jupiter - Saturn - Uranus - Neptune

Important Members

  • Sun: A star made of hot gases; its gravity holds the solar system together.
  • Earth: The known planet with life because it has suitable temperature, water, air, and protection from the atmosphere.
  • Moon: Earth's natural satellite; it reflects sunlight and shows phases.
  • Asteroids: Small rocky bodies, many found between Mars and Jupiter.
  • Comets: Icy bodies that can form a glowing tail near the Sun.
  • Meteoroids: Small space rocks; when they burn in Earth's atmosphere, they are seen as meteors.

Common Confusions

  • The Sun is a star, not a planet.
  • The Moon is a satellite, not a planet.
  • Planets do not produce their own light; they reflect sunlight.
  • Stars appear small because they are very far away.
  • Seasons are mainly due to Earth's tilted axis and revolution, not just distance from the Sun.

Classroom Connection

Use balls, lamps, chalk circles, and role-play to model the solar system. One child can stand as the Sun, others can revolve as planets, and a smaller child or ball can show the Moon revolving around Earth. Keep distances symbolic, not exact.

One-minute Revision

  • The solar system has the Sun at the centre and eight planets around it.
  • Planet order: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.
  • Inner planets are rocky; outer planets are giant and far away.
  • Rotation causes day and night; revolution is linked with year and seasons.
  • Asteroids, comets, satellites, dwarf planets, and meteoroids are also members of the solar system.
