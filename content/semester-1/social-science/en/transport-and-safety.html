Transport and Safety

This chapter revises means of transport, their social importance, traffic rules, road signs, child safety, and the teacher's role in building safe travel habits.

Importance Of Transport

  • Connects villages, towns, markets, schools, hospitals, workplaces, and families.
  • Supports agriculture by carrying seeds, fertilisers, machines, and crops to market.
  • Supports industry and trade by moving raw material and finished goods.
  • Helps during emergencies such as ambulance service, fire service, flood relief, and school evacuation.
  • Increases social contact, tourism, education, and national integration.

Safety Rules For Children

  • Walk on the footpath; if there is no footpath, walk on the side facing traffic.
  • Use zebra crossing, foot overbridge, or subway where available.
  • Look right-left-right before crossing and never cross while using a phone.
  • Wear helmet on two-wheelers and seat belt in cars; never stand at the bus door.
  • Do not play on roads, railway tracks, bridges, or near moving vehicles.
  • Wait for the bus in a line and board only after it stops completely.

Classroom Connection

Organise a school road-safety walk within campus or nearby safe space. Children can identify signs, unsafe points, bus waiting areas, and safe crossing habits. Connect this with EVS, social science, drawing, language, and mathematics through maps and data counts.

One-minute Revision

  • Transport moves people and goods by road, rail, water, air, and pipelines.
  • Transport supports economy, education, health, emergency work, and social contact.
  • Traffic rules protect pedestrians, passengers, drivers, and communities.
  • Children need repeated practice in safe crossing, helmet use, bus safety, and alert walking.
  • Teachers can make road safety practical through maps, role play, signs, and school safety drills.
