Microteaching and Basic Teaching Skills

Microteaching is a teacher-training technique in which a trainee practices one small teaching skill with a small group for a short time, receives feedback, improves, and reteaches. It develops confidence and mastery of basic teaching skills before real classroom teaching.

Meaning And Features Of Microteaching

  • Microteaching is a laboratory-like technique for training student-teachers.
  • It reduces the complexity of real teaching by limiting class size, time, content, and skill.
  • It focuses on one teaching skill at a time, such as introduction, questioning, explanation, reinforcement, or blackboard work.
  • It uses observation, feedback, re-planning, and reteaching for improvement.
  • It is safe for practice because mistakes are corrected before the trainee enters a full classroom.

Microteaching Cycle

Plan -> Teach -> Feedback -> Re-plan -> Re-teach -> Re-feedback
StepWhat happens
PlanningTrainee prepares a short micro-lesson for one skill and small content.
TeachingTrainee teaches a small group for about 5-10 minutes.
FeedbackSupervisor and peers discuss strengths and areas for improvement.
Re-planningTrainee modifies the lesson according to feedback.
Re-teachingTrainee teaches again using the improved plan.
Re-feedbackImprovement is discussed and skill mastery is strengthened.

Basic Teaching Skills

SkillMain indicatorsCommon mistake
Set induction / introductionUses previous knowledge, curiosity, example, question, object, story.Starting directly without preparing learners.
QuestioningClear, concise, graded, distributed, thought-provoking questions.Asking vague or only yes/no questions.
ExplanationSimple language, sequence, examples, comparison, recap.Long lecture with difficult words.
Stimulus variationVoice change, movement, gesture, pause, media, student activity.Same tone and position throughout.
ReinforcementPraise, nod, smile, repetition of correct answer, encouraging cue.Ignoring correct answers or insulting errors.
Illustration with examplesRelevant, local, simple, sufficient examples and non-examples.Examples not linked with the concept.
Blackboard writingReadable, straight, organized, key points, diagrams, spacing.Untidy, crowded, tiny, or misspelled writing.
ClosureSummary, key points, questions, link to next lesson.Ending suddenly when the bell rings.

Advantages And Limitations

AdvantagesLimitations
Builds confidence before real classroom teaching.Small artificial setting may not show all real classroom problems.
Focuses on one skill at a time.Skills may become mechanical if not later integrated.
Immediate feedback improves performance.Needs trained supervisor and observation time.
Reduces fear and develops reflective practice.Peer group may not respond like real children.

Classroom Connection

Before teaching Class 3 EVS, a trainee may practice only the skill of set induction: show a bottle of muddy water, ask "Would you drink this?", collect answers, and connect to the lesson "clean water." Peers observe whether the introduction created curiosity, linked previous knowledge, and led naturally to the lesson.

One-minute Revision

  • Microteaching is scaled-down teacher practice: small class, short time, small content, one skill.
  • Cycle: plan, teach, feedback, re-plan, re-teach, re-feedback.
  • Basic skills: set induction, questioning, explanation, stimulus variation, reinforcement, illustration, blackboard work, closure.
  • Microteaching develops confidence, skill mastery, feedback habit, and reflective teaching.
  • Final goal is integration of all skills in real classroom teaching.

Common Exam Lines

  • Microteaching was developed to train teachers in specific teaching skills under controlled conditions.
  • It follows a teach-feedback-reteach cycle.
  • Set induction prepares learners mentally for the new lesson.
  • Stimulus variation prevents monotony and maintains attention.
  • Reinforcement strengthens desirable learner responses.
