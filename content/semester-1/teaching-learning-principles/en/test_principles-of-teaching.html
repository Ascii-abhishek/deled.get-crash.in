Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What are principles of teaching?

Principles of teaching are broad and flexible guidelines based on psychology and classroom experience. They help teachers plan objectives, methods, activities, materials, interaction, feedback, and evaluation.

2. Write four major principles of teaching.

Four major principles are definite aim, child-centeredness, motivation, activity, individual differences, correlation, life connection, reinforcement, evaluation, and democratic behavior.

3. Why is the principle of individual differences important?

It is important because children differ in ability, pace, language, interest, background, confidence, and learning needs. A teacher should use varied tasks, peer support, flexible pace, and remedial help.

4. Distinguish between principles and maxims of teaching.

Principles are broad guidelines for the whole teaching process, while maxims are short practical rules for arranging content and explanation, such as known to unknown and simple to complex.

5. How does evaluation support teaching?

Evaluation checks whether learning objectives are achieved. It helps diagnose errors, give feedback, plan remedial teaching, and improve future teaching.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Explain the meaning and need of principles of teaching.

Principles of teaching are general rules that make teaching scientific, psychological, and effective. They guide the teacher in planning objectives, selecting content, choosing methods, using TLM, organizing activities, managing classroom interaction, giving feedback, and evaluating learning. They are needed because teaching without principles becomes random and teacher-centered. Principles help make teaching child-centered, purposeful, active, practical, inclusive, and connected with life.

2. Describe five important principles of teaching with examples.
  • Definite aim: teacher decides what learners will learn.
  • Child-centeredness: teacher uses methods according to age and level.
  • Activity: learners learn by doing, drawing, measuring, and discussing.
  • Motivation: teacher creates interest through stories, questions, and problems.
  • Evaluation: teacher checks learning through oral questions, observation, and worksheets.
3. Explain the psychological basis of teaching principles.

Teaching principles are supported by educational psychology. Readiness says learning is better when the child is prepared. Interest and motivation increase attention. Practice strengthens learning. Association connects new learning with old knowledge. Reinforcement strengthens correct responses. Feedback helps correction. Individual differences remind the teacher that learners vary in ability, language, experience, speed, and confidence.

4. How can a teacher apply teaching principles in an elementary classroom?
  • Begin with clear objectives and previous knowledge.
  • Use motivating questions or local examples.
  • Make children observe, speak, write, draw, measure, and solve.
  • Use TLM according to the concept.
  • Give varied tasks for different learners.
  • Connect learning with daily life and other subjects.
  • Check learning and give feedback.
  • Maintain a respectful democratic classroom.
5. Discuss child-centeredness, activity, and evaluation as principles of teaching.

Child-centeredness means teaching should suit the learner's age, level, pace, language, and interest. Activity means children should learn by doing, observing, discussing, experimenting, and practicing. Evaluation means continuous checking of learning through questions, observation, worksheets, and feedback. Together these principles shift teaching from teacher talk to meaningful learner participation.
