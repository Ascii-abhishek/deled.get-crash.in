Communication

This chapter explains communication as the exchange of ideas, facts, feelings, and instructions in a way that the receiver understands the intended meaning. In teaching, communication is the bridge between teacher, learner, and content.

Exam Snapshot

Term Last-minute meaning Classroom example
Communication Exchange of message, idea, information, or feeling with shared understanding. A teacher explains a task and students understand what to do.
Sender Person who sends the message. Teacher giving instructions for group work.
Receiver Person who receives and interprets the message. Students listening and following the instruction.
Feedback Response that shows whether the message was understood. A child answers, asks a doubt, or performs the task correctly.
Noise Any barrier that disturbs communication. Loud classroom, unclear language, fear, or poor blackboard writing.

Meaning Of Communication

  • Communication means sending and receiving messages so that meaning is shared correctly.
  • It may happen through words, gestures, facial expressions, pictures, symbols, writing, charts, or digital media.
  • In teaching, communication is not one-way talking; it includes listening, questioning, response, feedback, and correction.
  • Good communication helps students understand the meaning, purpose, and use of the lesson.
  • The Hindi word sam + preshan suggests sending a message properly so the receiver understands it correctly.

Need And Importance Of Communication In Teaching

  • Clarifies content: It makes new knowledge understandable.
  • Creates attention: Clear voice, examples, questions, and eye contact hold student attention.
  • Motivates learners: Encouraging words and relevant examples build interest.
  • Supports participation: Students speak, ask, discuss, and respond.
  • Removes doubts: Feedback tells the teacher what needs re-explanation.
  • Builds classroom relationship: Respectful language creates trust and confidence.
  • Improves evaluation: Teacher can assess learning through oral responses, written work, and behavior.

Communication Process

Sender -> Message -> Encoding -> Channel -> Receiver -> Decoding -> Feedback
                         ^                                  |
                         |------------ Noise ---------------|
Element Meaning Classroom example
Sender Source of the message. Teacher or student speaker.
Message Idea, fact, instruction, question, or feeling. "Open page 12 and underline the nouns."
Encoding Putting thought into words, symbols, gestures, or pictures. Teacher draws a diagram to explain water cycle.
Channel Medium used to carry the message. Voice, blackboard, chart, worksheet, video.
Receiver Person for whom the message is meant. Learner or group of learners.
Decoding Understanding the message. Student understands the steps of subtraction.
Feedback Receiver's response. Answer, doubt, facial expression, completed task.

Types Of Communication

Type Meaning Classroom use
Verbal Communication through spoken or written words. Explanation, question-answer, reading, written instruction.
Non-verbal Communication without words. Gesture, smile, eye contact, posture, pointing to a chart.
Oral Spoken communication. Storytelling, recitation, discussion, announcement.
Written Messages through writing. Blackboard work, homework note, worksheet, test feedback.
One-way Message flows from sender to receiver without immediate response. Notice, announcement, short instruction.
Two-way Message and feedback both flow. Question-answer, discussion, peer explanation.

Effective Classroom Communication

  • Use simple, correct, and age-appropriate language.
  • Keep the message clear, short, and logically ordered.
  • Link new ideas with children's previous knowledge and local examples.
  • Use voice modulation, pause, eye contact, gestures, and blackboard work.
  • Use charts, pictures, real objects, stories, activities, and examples.
  • Ask open and closed questions according to the objective.
  • Listen carefully to learners' answers and doubts.
  • Give immediate, respectful, and corrective feedback.
  • Check understanding through quick tasks, not only through "Did you understand?"

Barriers To Communication

Barrier How it affects learning Teacher remedy
Physical noise Students cannot hear or focus. Seat arrangement, silence signal, clear voice.
Difficult language Message is not understood. Use simple language and examples.
Unclear message Students become confused. Give stepwise instructions and write key points.
Fear or hesitation Children avoid asking doubts. Create respectful, democratic classroom climate.
Lack of feedback Teacher does not know whether learners understood. Ask questions, observe work, and use quick checks.
Poor listening Teacher misses student needs and misconceptions. Listen actively and respond patiently.

Teacher As An Effective Communicator

  • Plans what to say, how to say it, and which examples or materials to use.
  • Uses both verbal and non-verbal communication carefully.
  • Encourages learners to speak without fear.
  • Balances explanation with activity and feedback.
  • Uses inclusive language and avoids insult, sarcasm, bias, or labeling.
  • Adapts communication for children with different language backgrounds and learning needs.

Classroom Connection

If a Class 2 teacher says, "Do this quickly," many children may remain confused. If the teacher says, "First write today's date. Then copy the two words from the board. After that draw one picture for each word," the message becomes clear. Good classroom communication converts vague instruction into visible action.

One-minute Revision

  • Communication means exchange of message with shared understanding.
  • Main elements: sender, message, encoding, channel, receiver, decoding, feedback, and noise.
  • Teaching needs two-way communication, not only teacher talk.
  • Types: verbal, non-verbal, oral, written, one-way, and two-way.
  • Effective communication needs clarity, simple language, proper channel, feedback, listening, and respect.
  • Barriers include noise, difficult language, unclear message, fear, poor listening, and lack of feedback.

Common Exam Lines

  • Communication is the process of exchanging ideas, facts, feelings, and messages so that the receiver understands the intended meaning.
  • Feedback is essential because it tells whether the message has been understood.
  • In classroom teaching, communication should be clear, simple, purposeful, timely, and learner-centered.
  • Non-verbal communication such as eye contact, facial expression, gesture, and posture strongly affects classroom learning.
  • Effective communication makes teaching active, participatory, and meaningful.
