Principles of Teaching

This chapter revises the basic principles that make teaching planned, meaningful, child-centered, and result-oriented. For exams, remember that principles guide the teacher in selecting aims, content, methods, materials, activities, and evaluation.

Meaning Of Teaching Principles

  • Principles of teaching are general rules or guidelines based on psychology, experience, and classroom practice.
  • They help the teacher decide what to teach, why to teach, how to teach, and how to check learning.
  • They do not work like rigid formulas; a good teacher applies them according to learner level and situation.
  • Good teaching principles make the classroom active, inclusive, practical, and learner-friendly.

Psychological Basis Of Teaching Principles

  • Readiness: A child learns better when mentally and physically ready.
  • Interest: Interesting material receives more attention.
  • Practice: Regular and meaningful practice strengthens learning.
  • Association: New knowledge is understood better when linked with old knowledge.
  • Feedback: Correction and encouragement guide future learning.
  • Individual difference: Learners differ in speed, language, experience, ability, and confidence.

How A Teacher Applies Principles

  • Start the lesson with a clear objective and a motivating situation.
  • Check previous knowledge before introducing a new idea.
  • Use examples from the child's home, school, village, market, or local environment.
  • Give children opportunities to observe, ask, speak, write, draw, manipulate, and solve.
  • Use TLM according to the concept, not as decoration.
  • Use questions to develop thinking, not only recall.
  • Give immediate feedback and plan remedial teaching for errors.
  • Keep classroom climate respectful, inclusive, and democratic.

Classroom Connection

In a Class 4 lesson on fractions, a teacher can apply several principles together: begin with sharing a roti or paper circle, ask children what they already know, let them fold and shade parts, connect fractions with daily life, give different practice tasks, and end with quick questions. The lesson becomes purposeful, active, concrete, child-centered, and evaluative.

One-minute Revision

  • Teaching principles are flexible guidelines for effective teaching.
  • Major principles: definite aim, child-centeredness, motivation, activity, life connection, individual differences, correlation, reinforcement, evaluation, and democratic behavior.
  • Principles are broader than maxims; maxims are short teaching rules.
  • Teaching principles are based on readiness, interest, practice, association, feedback, and individual differences.
  • A principle is useful only when the teacher applies it according to classroom needs.

Common Exam Lines

  • Principles of teaching are general guidelines that make teaching scientific, psychological, and effective.
  • Good teaching should proceed from learner needs, previous knowledge, activity, motivation, and evaluation.
  • The principle of individual differences reminds the teacher that every child cannot learn at the same speed and in the same way.
  • Evaluation is not only for marks; it is also for improving teaching and learning.
  • Teaching should be connected with life so that knowledge becomes meaningful and useful.
