Maxims of Teaching

Maxims of teaching are short practical rules that help the teacher arrange content from easy to difficult and from familiar experience to new knowledge. They make classroom explanation natural, psychological, and learner-friendly.

Exam Snapshot

MaximMeaningClassroom example
Known to unknownStart from what learners already know.Teach transport using bicycle and bus before metro or airplane.
Simple to complexMove from easy ideas to difficult ideas.Teach single-digit addition before carrying.
Concrete to abstractUse real objects before symbols and ideas.Use seeds before writing 6 + 4 = 10.
Particular to generalUse examples first, then form rule.Show many nouns, then define noun.
Whole to partShow the complete thing before details.Read the whole poem, then discuss lines and words.

Meaning And Need

  • Maxims are time-tested teaching rules used to sequence lesson content.
  • They are based on how children naturally learn: from experience to idea, observation to rule, and action to symbol.
  • They reduce confusion, maintain interest, and help the teacher move step by step.
  • They are not rigid laws; a teacher selects the maxim according to topic, age, learner level, and classroom context.

Main Maxims Of Teaching

MaximTeacher actionWhy useful
Known to unknownBegin from familiar home, school, local, or previous learning examples.New learning becomes less frightening and more meaningful.
Simple to complexTeach easy points before difficult combinations.Learner confidence grows gradually.
Concrete to abstractUse real objects, pictures, models, and activities before symbols.Primary children understand concrete experience better.
Easy to difficultGive easy questions first, then higher-level questions.Prevents failure and builds readiness.
Particular to generalGive examples first, then guide learners to a rule.Rules become understood, not memorized blindly.
General to particularState a rule, then apply it to examples.Useful when learners already have sufficient background.
Whole to partPresent the complete object, story, poem, map, or picture before details.Learners see the overall meaning first.
Part to wholeTeach units or components and combine them later.Useful in alphabet, word building, and skill practice.
Indefinite to definiteClarify vague experience into exact concepts and terms.Children move from rough idea to clear understanding.
Empirical to rationalBegin with observation and experience, then explain reasons.Supports scientific thinking.

Frequently Confused Maxims

PairDifferenceExample
Concrete to abstract vs simple to complexConcrete/abstract is about material type; simple/complex is about difficulty level.Seeds to number symbol; 2+3 before 27+38.
Particular to general vs general to particularFirst moves examples to rule; second moves rule to examples.Noun examples to definition; definition to sentence practice.
Whole to part vs part to wholeFirst shows full meaning first; second builds from units.Whole story to events; letters to words.
Known to unknown vs easy to difficultKnown/unknown depends on familiarity; easy/difficult depends on complexity.Village well to water cycle; small sums to large sums.

Using Maxims In Lesson Planning

  • Check previous knowledge before introducing new content.
  • Begin with concrete objects, pictures, activities, or local examples in primary classes.
  • Arrange questions from easy to difficult.
  • Use examples before abstract definitions when teaching language and EVS concepts.
  • Show the whole poem, story, map, or model before detailed analysis when needed.
  • Do not force one maxim everywhere; combine maxims according to the lesson.

Classroom Connection

To teach "triangle" in Class 3, a teacher can show a real triangular signboard or paper shape, ask children to find similar shapes in the room, count sides and corners, and then give the definition. This uses concrete to abstract, known to unknown, particular to general, and simple to complex together.

One-minute Revision

  • Maxims are short practical teaching rules.
  • They guide sequence: known to unknown, simple to complex, concrete to abstract, easy to difficult.
  • They help children move from experience to idea and from example to rule.
  • No maxim is universal; use according to topic and learner level.
  • Primary teaching usually needs more concrete, activity-based, and familiar examples.

Common Exam Lines

  • Maxims of teaching make teaching psychological, systematic, and easy for learners.
  • The maxim known to unknown is based on the use of previous knowledge.
  • The maxim concrete to abstract is very important at the primary level.
  • Particular to general is inductive; general to particular is deductive.
  • A good teacher uses several maxims together instead of following one mechanically.
