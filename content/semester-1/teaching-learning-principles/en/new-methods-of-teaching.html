New Methods of Teaching

New methods of teaching make learning child-centered, activity-based, participatory, problem-oriented, and connected with life. They reduce rote learning and help children learn through experience, inquiry, cooperation, creativity, and reflection.

Exam Snapshot

MethodCore ideaElementary example
Activity-based learningChildren learn by doing meaningful tasks.Sorting leaves by shape and size in EVS.
Project methodLearners investigate a real problem and create an outcome.Making a chart on local water sources.
Play-way methodLearning through games, play, rhythm, and joyful tasks.Number games for addition.
Problem-solving methodLearning by identifying and solving a problem.Finding ways to keep the classroom clean.
Cooperative learningChildren learn in small groups with shared responsibility.Group reading and peer explanation.

Need For New Methods

  • Traditional lecture alone often makes children passive.
  • Primary children learn better through activity, concrete experience, language use, and interaction.
  • New methods support curiosity, creativity, cooperation, problem solving, confidence, and self-learning.
  • They align with child-centered and inclusive education.

Major New Methods

MethodSteps / featuresPrecaution
Activity-based learningObjective, material, activity, discussion, conclusion, feedback.Activity must serve learning, not become only entertainment.
Project methodSelect problem, plan, collect information, work, present, evaluate.Project should be age-appropriate and not done only by adults.
Play-way methodUse games, songs, stories, movement, puzzles, and role play.Keep rules clear and linked with objective.
Problem-solving methodRecognize problem, suggest solutions, test, conclude.Teacher should guide without giving the answer too quickly.
Heuristic methodChildren discover through questions, observation, and inquiry.Requires time, materials, and careful guidance.
Cooperative learningSmall groups with roles, shared work, peer help, group presentation.Ensure every child participates, not only strong learners.
BrainstormingMany ideas are collected first; evaluation happens later.Do not criticize ideas during idea generation.
ICT-supported learningUse audio, video, digital pictures, quizzes, and simulations where available.Technology should support, not replace, teacher interaction.

Traditional And New Methods

Traditional emphasisNew method emphasis
Teacher talks; learners listen.Learners observe, do, discuss, and create.
Content completion is central.Understanding and application are central.
Same task for all children.Flexible activities and group roles.
Memory-based answers.Problem solving, inquiry, creativity, and reflection.
Evaluation mostly at the end.Continuous feedback during learning.

Teacher's Role In New Methods

  • Planner of meaningful activities and projects.
  • Facilitator who guides rather than dominates.
  • Questioner who encourages inquiry and thinking.
  • Observer who notices participation and difficulties.
  • Evaluator who gives feedback and supports improvement.
  • Inclusive organizer who ensures every child gets a role.

Classroom Connection

To teach "plants need water," a teacher can divide the class into groups, give two small plants, water one regularly and keep one without water for observation, ask children to record changes, discuss reasons, and draw conclusions. This combines activity-based, problem-solving, cooperative, and heuristic approaches.

One-minute Revision

  • New methods are child-centered, active, participatory, and life-connected.
  • Important methods: activity-based, project, play-way, problem-solving, heuristic, cooperative, brainstorming, and ICT-supported learning.
  • Teacher becomes facilitator, guide, organizer, and evaluator.
  • New methods develop understanding, creativity, cooperation, and problem-solving.
  • Method choice depends on objective, topic, class level, time, and resources.

Common Exam Lines

  • New methods shift teaching from teacher-centered lecture to learner-centered activity.
  • Activity-based learning is based on learning by doing.
  • Project method connects school learning with real-life problems.
  • Play-way method is especially useful at the primary level.
  • Cooperative learning develops academic learning as well as social skills.
