Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define reproduction in plants.

Reproduction is the process by which organisms produce new individuals of their own kind. Plants reproduce by asexual and sexual methods.

2. Write two important terms from this chapter.

In flowering plants, pollination is followed by fertilization, seed formation, fruit formation, and dispersal. Vegetative propagation occurs through root, stem, leaf, and bud.

3. Give two classroom examples related to reproduction in plants.

Vegetative propagation is useful because it produces many plants quickly from one parent plant. Flower has sepals, petals, stamens, and pistil.

4. Why is observation important in this chapter?

5. Write one common misconception and its correction.

In flowering plants, pollination is followed by fertilization, seed formation, fruit formation, and dispersal. Seed dispersal helps plants avoid overcrowding and reach new habitats.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the main concepts of Reproduction in Plants with examples.

Reproduction in Plants: Reproduction in plants explains how new plants are formed by seeds, spores, vegetative parts, and flowers. At the elementary level, the chapter should be taught with germination, flower observation, and local examples.

  • Vegetative reproduction uses root, stem, leaf, or bud; it is fast and keeps parent characters.
  • Budding in yeast and spore formation in fungi or ferns are simple asexual methods.
  • Sexual reproduction in flowering plants uses male and female reproductive parts.
  • Artificial methods such as cutting, grafting, layering, and tissue culture help horticulture.

Children can germinate gram seeds on moist cotton and record daily changes: seed coat softens, radicle emerges, plumule grows, and first leaves appear. A hibiscus flower can be observed with a hand lens to identify sepals, petals, stamen, and pistil without damaging many flowers.

Reproduction in Plants: Reproduction in plants explains how new plants are formed by seeds, spores, vegetative parts, and flowers. At the elementary level, the chapter should be taught with germination, flower observation, and local examples.
