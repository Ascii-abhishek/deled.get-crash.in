Model Preparation

This chapter prepares D.El.Ed students to design one simple science model or TLM that explains a concept clearly, safely and economically to primary children.

Why Models Are Used

  • Models make abstract or large processes visible in the classroom.
  • They encourage observation, questioning, group work and presentation.
  • Low-cost models help schools with limited resources.
  • A good model connects concept, activity and assessment.

Possible Model Themes

  • Rainwater harvesting: roof, pipe, filter, storage tank and recharge pit.
  • Laws of motion: toy car, ramp, different loads and measured distance.
  • Electromagnetic force: simple electric bell or nail electromagnet.
  • States of matter: particle arrangement using beads or bottle caps.
  • Types of force: stations for muscular, magnetic, frictional and gravitational force.

Steps In Model Preparation

  • Select one concept and write the learning objective.
  • List safe, low-cost local materials.
  • Draw a rough plan with labels before making.
  • Prepare, test, improve and then label the model neatly.
  • Write a short explanation: aim, materials, procedure, science principle, precautions and classroom use.

Evaluation Points

  • Scientific correctness: model must explain the concept accurately.
  • Clarity: labels and arrows should be readable.
  • Safety: no exposed wire, sharp edge or unsafe chemical.
  • Participation: children should be able to touch, ask and explain.
  • Economy: prefer waste or local material when suitable.

Concepts And Differences

PairDifference
Model vs chartA model may be three-dimensional or working; a chart is mainly two-dimensional visual support.
Working vs static modelWorking model shows a process; static model shows structure or arrangement.
Project vs modelProject includes investigation and report; model is a teaching representation that may be part of a project.
Decoration vs learning valueDecoration looks attractive; learning value makes the concept clearer.

Quick Diagram / Memory Hook

Good model = Concept + Safe material + Clear labels + Child activity + Explanation

Classroom Connection

For rainwater harvesting, children can pour water on a sloped cardboard roof and watch it pass through a pipe into a bottle tank. The teacher links it with local water scarcity and asks children to suggest school-level water-saving steps.

One-minute Revision

  • A model is a simplified representation used for learning.
  • Choose one concept and one clear learning objective.
  • Use safe, low-cost and locally available materials.
  • Labels, arrows and explanation are as important as appearance.
  • Assessment should check concept clarity, safety, participation and classroom use.

Common Exam Lines

  • A science model should not be only decorative; it must clarify a scientific concept.
  • Model preparation develops creativity, scientific attitude and teaching skill in trainee teachers.
  • Every model should include aim, materials, procedure, principle, precautions and use in class.
