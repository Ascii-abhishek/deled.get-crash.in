Reproduction in Plants

Reproduction in plants explains how new plants are formed by seeds, spores, vegetative parts, and flowers. At the elementary level, the chapter should be taught with germination, flower observation, and local examples.

Modes Of Reproduction

  • Vegetative reproduction uses root, stem, leaf, or bud; it is fast and keeps parent characters.
  • Budding in yeast and spore formation in fungi or ferns are simple asexual methods.
  • Sexual reproduction in flowering plants uses male and female reproductive parts.
  • Artificial methods such as cutting, grafting, layering, and tissue culture help horticulture.
  • Seeds protect the embryo and help the plant spread to new places.

Flower Parts And Their Work

  • Sepals protect the flower bud.
  • Petals attract insects and other pollinators.
  • Stamen is the male part; anther produces pollen grains.
  • Pistil or carpel is the female part; stigma receives pollen, style connects, ovary contains ovules.
  • After fertilization, ovule becomes seed and ovary becomes fruit.

Pollination, Fertilization, And Dispersal

  • Self-pollination occurs within the same flower or same plant.
  • Cross-pollination occurs between flowers of different plants of the same kind.
  • Fertilization is fusion of male and female gametes to form zygote.
  • Dispersal by wind, water, animals, and bursting reduces competition and helps survival.
  • Germination needs water, air, suitable temperature, and a healthy seed.

Concepts And Differences

ConceptMeaning / use
Asexual reproductionOne parent; no gamete fusion; offspring mostly identical.
Sexual reproductionGamete fusion; seeds formed; variation possible.

Cross-pollinationPollen reaches stigma of another plant of same species.

Classroom Connection

Children can germinate gram seeds on moist cotton and record daily changes: seed coat softens, radicle emerges, plumule grows, and first leaves appear. A hibiscus flower can be observed with a hand lens to identify sepals, petals, stamen, and pistil without damaging many flowers.

One-minute Revision

  • Plants reproduce by asexual and sexual methods.
  • Vegetative propagation occurs through root, stem, leaf, and bud.
  • Flower has sepals, petals, stamens, and pistil.
  • Pollination comes before fertilization; fertilization forms seed.
  • Seed dispersal helps plants avoid overcrowding and reach new habitats.

Common Exam Lines

  • Reproduction is the process by which organisms produce new individuals of their own kind.
  • In flowering plants, pollination is followed by fertilization, seed formation, fruit formation, and dispersal.
  • Vegetative propagation is useful because it produces many plants quickly from one parent plant.
