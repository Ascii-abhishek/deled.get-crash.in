Living Things

This chapter helps future primary teachers explain how we identify living things, classify natural and human-made objects, and compare plants, animals, and non-living things through observation.

Characteristics Of Living Things

  • Living things need food for energy and growth.
  • They respire: plants and animals use oxygen to release energy from food.
  • They grow and show changes in size, shape, or maturity.
  • They respond to stimuli such as light, touch, sound, smell, and temperature.
  • They reproduce, excrete waste, and finally die.

Classification Around Us

  • Natural living: plants, animals, birds, insects, microbes.
  • Natural non-living: sunlight, air, water, rocks, soil.
  • Human-made non-living: classroom furniture, books, tools, machines.
  • Some things confuse children: a seed seems inactive but can germinate; fire moves and grows but is non-living because it has no cells or reproduction.

Plants And Animals

  • Plants usually make food by photosynthesis and stay fixed in one place.
  • Animals usually move from place to place and depend on plants or other animals for food.
  • Both plants and animals respire, grow, reproduce, respond, and need suitable surroundings.
  • Microorganisms are also living though they cannot be seen with naked eyes.

Concepts And Differences

ConceptMeaning / use
Living thingsHave cells and life processes; grow from inside; reproduce.
Non-living thingsNo cells or life processes; do not reproduce naturally.
PlantsMostly fixed; prepare food; have roots, stems, leaves.
AnimalsMostly move; depend on other organisms for food; have sense organs.

Classroom Connection

Ask children to collect ten safe objects from the school campus and sort them into living, non-living, natural, and human-made groups. Then discuss confusing cases such as seed, dry leaf, wooden desk, and milk. This develops observation and scientific reasoning.

One-minute Revision

  • Living things show nutrition, respiration, growth, response, reproduction, excretion, and death.
  • All moving things are not living; all still things are not non-living.
  • A seed is living because it can germinate under suitable conditions.
  • Plants and animals are both living but differ in food-making and movement.
  • Classification should be based on evidence, not appearance alone.

Common Exam Lines

  • Living things are identified by life processes, not by movement alone.
  • Plants and animals both need air, water, food or nutrients, and suitable habitat.
  • A good science teacher encourages children to classify objects after observation and discussion.
