Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Define living things.

Living things are identified by life processes, not by movement alone. Living things show nutrition, respiration, growth, response, reproduction, excretion, and death.

2. Write two important terms from this chapter.

Plants and animals both need air, water, food or nutrients, and suitable habitat. All moving things are not living; all still things are not non-living.

3. Give two classroom examples related to living things.

A good science teacher encourages children to classify objects after observation and discussion. A seed is living because it can germinate under suitable conditions.

4. Why is observation important in this chapter?

Living things are identified by life processes, not by movement alone. Plants and animals are both living but differ in food-making and movement.

5. Write one common misconception and its correction.

Plants and animals both need air, water, food or nutrients, and suitable habitat. Classification should be based on evidence, not appearance alone.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the main concepts of Living Things with examples.

Living Things: This chapter helps future primary teachers explain how we identify living things, classify natural and human-made objects, and compare plants, animals, and non-living things through observation.

  • Living things need food for energy and growth.
  • They respire: plants and animals use oxygen to release energy from food.
  • They grow and show changes in size, shape, or maturity.
  • They respond to stimuli such as light, touch, sound, smell, and temperature.

Ask children to collect ten safe objects from the school campus and sort them into living, non-living, natural, and human-made groups. Then discuss confusing cases such as seed, dry leaf, wooden desk, and milk. This develops observation and scientific reasoning.

2. Describe how you will teach this chapter through activities in an elementary classroom.

Living Things: This chapter helps future primary teachers explain how we identify living things, classify natural and human-made objects, and compare plants, animals, and non-living things through observation.

  • Natural living: plants, animals, birds, insects, microbes.
  • Natural non-living: sunlight, air, water, rocks, soil.
  • Human-made non-living: classroom furniture, books, tools, machines.
  • Some things confuse children: a seed seems inactive but can germinate; fire moves and grows but is non-living because it has no cells or reproduction.

Ask children to collect ten safe objects from the school campus and sort them into living, non-living, natural, and human-made groups. Then discuss confusing cases such as seed, dry leaf, wooden desk, and milk. This develops observation and scientific reasoning.

3. Prepare a comparison table of two important ideas from this chapter.

Living Things: This chapter helps future primary teachers explain how we identify living things, classify natural and human-made objects, and compare plants, animals, and non-living things through observation.

  • Plants usually make food by photosynthesis and stay fixed in one place.
  • Animals usually move from place to place and depend on plants or other animals for food.
  • Both plants and animals respire, grow, reproduce, respond, and need suitable surroundings.
  • Microorganisms are also living though they cannot be seen with naked eyes.
