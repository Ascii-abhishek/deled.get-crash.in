Pronunciation and Lesson Planning

This crash course links correct English pronunciation with practical lesson planning for primary classes. Focus on phonemes, stress, intonation, silent letters, reading aloud, and the parts of a strong English lesson plan.

Core Points

  • Pronunciation teaching begins with listening before speaking; learners first notice the sound, then imitate it, then use it in words and sentences.
  • A lesson plan should connect previous knowledge, clear objectives, presentation, practice, evaluation, homework, and remedial support.
  • For young learners, pronunciation practice works best through rhymes, chants, dialogues, picture cards, and teacher modelling.
  • A good English lesson plan includes enough student talk time, not only teacher explanation.
  • Assessment of pronunciation should note clarity, stress, rhythm, and confidence rather than punishing accent differences.
  • Silent letters, similar sounds, and spelling-sound mismatch need repeated short practice.

Quick Memory Hook

Listen -> Say -> Use -> Check -> Correct.

Classroom Connection

For Class 4, teach silent letters with picture cards: knee, knife, write, walk. Let children circle the silent letter, listen to the teacher, repeat in pairs, read a sentence, and then make one new sentence. End by asking three learners to pronounce and explain one word each.

One-minute Revision

  • Phoneme: Smallest sound unit that changes meaning, such as /p/ and /b/.
  • Vowel sound: Open sound made without full blockage of air.
  • Consonant sound: Sound made with partial or complete obstruction of air.
  • Stress: Extra force on a syllable or word.
  • Intonation: Rise and fall of voice in sentences.
  • Silent letter: Letter written but not pronounced, as in walk or knife.
