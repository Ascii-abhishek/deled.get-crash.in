Food, Health and Disease

Revise nutrients, balanced diet, deficiency diseases, communicable diseases, sanitation, immunisation, and health education.

Exam Snapshot

TermFast meaningExample
FoodSubstance eaten for energy, growth, repair, and health.Cereals, pulses, milk, vegetables
NutrientUseful component of food needed by body.Carbohydrate, protein, vitamin
Balanced dietDiet containing all nutrients, water, and fibre in proper amount.Dal, roti, rice, vegetables, curd
Deficiency diseaseDisease caused by lack of a nutrient.Rickets, anaemia
Communicable diseaseDisease that spreads from one person to another.Measles, cholera
ImmunisationGiving vaccine to protect from disease.Polio drops
SanitationClean habits and safe disposal of waste.Handwashing, toilet use

Key Points

  • Carbohydrates and fats give energy; proteins help growth and repair; vitamins and minerals protect and regulate body functions.
  • Water and roughage are essential for digestion, temperature balance, and waste removal.
  • Balanced diet depends on age, work, health, local food, and economic condition.
  • Communicable diseases spread through air, water, food, insects, touch, and body fluids.
  • Prevention includes clean water, handwashing, covered food, toilets, mosquito control, vaccination, and early treatment.
  • A teacher should notice health signs, promote hygiene routines, and avoid stigma towards ill children.

Concepts And Differences

First ideaSecond ideaMain difference
CommunicableNon-communicableCommunicable spreads from person to person; non-communicable does not spread by contact.
MalnutritionBalanced dietMalnutrition is lack/excess/imbalance; balanced diet provides proper nutrients.
VaccineMedicineVaccine prevents disease; medicine treats after illness.
HygieneSanitationHygiene is personal cleanliness; sanitation is community cleanliness and waste management.

Classroom Connection

  • Begin from learners own experience and then place the key vocabulary in a short board summary.
  • Use pictures, local material, story, role play, measurement, observation, or group discussion as suitable.
  • Support weak readers with oral cues, peer help, and small steps.
  • Close with a two-minute recap and one short written question.
