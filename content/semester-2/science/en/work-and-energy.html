Work and Energy

Revise work, energy, power, forms of energy, sources, transformation, conservation, and classroom examples.

Exam Snapshot

TermFast meaningExample
WorkWork is done when force causes displacement in its direction.Pushing a desk that moves
EnergyCapacity to do work.Food gives energy to play
PowerRate of doing work or using energy.Fast motor has more power
Kinetic energyEnergy due to motion.Moving ball
Potential energyStored energy due to position or condition.Water stored at height
Renewable sourceSource naturally replenished.Solar, wind, flowing water
Conservation of energyEnergy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.Electricity to light and heat

Key Points

  • Work in science needs both force and displacement; holding a heavy bag without movement is tiring but not scientific work.
  • Energy appears in many forms: mechanical, heat, light, sound, electrical, chemical, and solar energy.
  • Energy transformation is common: torch changes chemical energy of cell into light and heat.
  • Renewable sources reduce dependence on fossil fuels and pollution.
  • Non-renewable sources like coal, petroleum, and natural gas are limited and cause pollution when burned.
  • Energy conservation means using energy carefully: switch off appliances, use efficient devices, reduce waste, and use public transport.

Concepts And Differences

First ideaSecond ideaMain difference
WorkEnergyWork is action of force causing displacement; energy is capacity to do work.
Kinetic energyPotential energyKinetic is due to motion; potential is stored due to position or condition.
RenewableNon-renewableRenewable replenishes naturally; non-renewable is limited.
Energy transformationEnergy conservationTransformation changes form; conservation means total energy remains constant and should be used wisely.

Classroom Connection

  • Begin from learners own experience and then place the key vocabulary in a short board summary.
  • Use pictures, local material, story, role play, measurement, observation, or group discussion as suitable.
  • Support weak readers with oral cues, peer help, and small steps.
  • Close with a two-minute recap and one short written question.
