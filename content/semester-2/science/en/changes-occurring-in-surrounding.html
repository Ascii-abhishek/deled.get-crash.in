Changes Occurring in Surrounding

Revise physical and chemical changes, reversible and irreversible changes, heat exchange, expansion, contraction, evaporation, condensation, and daily-life examples around the child.

Exam Snapshot

TermFast meaningExample
ChangeAny difference in shape, size, state, position, colour, temperature, or composition.Melting ice, rusting iron, cooking food
Physical changeChange in size, shape, state, or appearance without forming a new substance.Melting wax, tearing paper
Chemical changeChange in which a new substance with new properties is formed.Burning paper, souring milk
Reversible changeChange that can be brought back nearly to the original state.Freezing and melting water
Irreversible changeChange that cannot be fully reversed by simple means.Cooking rice, burning wood
ExpansionIncrease in size due to heating.Railway tracks need gaps
ContractionDecrease in size due to cooling.Tight metal lid loosens in hot water

Key Points

  • Changes may be slow or fast, natural or man-made, periodic or non-periodic.
  • No new substance is formed in most physical changes; many are reversible.
  • Chemical changes usually involve new substance, heat/light, gas, smell, colour change, or precipitate.
  • Heat flows from a hotter body to a colder body until temperature becomes balanced.
  • Water cycle is a chain of evaporation, condensation, cloud formation, and rain.
  • Teachers should ask children to classify local examples instead of memorising definitions only.

Important Steps And Applications

  • Begin with the definition.
  • Give a daily-life example.
  • Write cause or process in small steps.
  • Add safety and classroom application.

Classroom Connection

  • Ask children to collect examples from home, kitchen, field, road, and school.
  • Use small-group observation, classification, reason-giving, and safety writing.
  • Clarify concepts through low-cost material, pictures, tables, and oral discussion.
  • Close with a two-minute recap and one written answer on a local example.
