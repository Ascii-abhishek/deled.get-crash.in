Classification of Elements

Revise atom, element, molecule, valency, symbols, formulae, metals, non-metals, modern classification, and chemical reactions.

Exam Snapshot

TermFast meaningExample
ElementPure substance made of one kind of atom.Iron, oxygen, carbon
AtomSmallest unit of an element taking part in chemical change.Hydrogen atom
MoleculeGroup of atoms chemically joined.O2, H2O
SymbolShort representation of an element.Na for sodium
ValencyCombining capacity of an atom.Hydrogen valency 1, oxygen 2
FormulaSymbolic representation of a compound.H2O, CO2
Periodic tableArrangement of elements according to atomic number and properties.Groups and periods

Key Points

  • Elements are classified to study many substances systematically and predict properties.
  • Metals are generally lustrous, malleable, ductile, good conductors, and form basic oxides; non-metals show opposite properties and often form acidic oxides.
  • Metalloids show mixed properties and are useful in electronics, for example silicon.
  • Modern periodic table arranges elements in increasing atomic number; elements in same group have similar properties.
  • Valency helps write chemical formulae: cross the valencies and simplify if needed.
  • Chemical reaction forms new substances and is represented by word equation or chemical equation.

Concepts And Differences

First ideaSecond ideaMain difference
MetalNon-metalMetal conducts heat/electricity and is malleable; non-metal usually poor conductor and brittle if solid.
AtomMoleculeAtom is smallest unit of element; molecule is group of atoms bonded together.
ElementCompoundElement has one kind of atom; compound has two or more elements chemically combined.
GroupPeriodGroup is vertical column; period is horizontal row in periodic table.

Classroom Connection

  • Begin from learners own experience and then place the key vocabulary in a short board summary.
  • Use pictures, local material, story, role play, measurement, observation, or group discussion as suitable.
  • Support weak readers with oral cues, peer help, and small steps.
  • Close with a two-minute recap and one short written question.
